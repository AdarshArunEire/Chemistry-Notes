Oxidation & Reduction

Oxidation & Reduction

At a glance

Redox at a glance

Balancing by oxidation numbers — core method

  1. Assign oxidation numbers to all elements.
  2. Pick out only the elements whose oxidation numbers change.
  3. Write the electron-loss / electron-gain changes mathematically.
  4. Equalise electrons gained and lost to get the molar ratio.
  5. Put the ratio into the original equation and balance the rest by inspection.
  6. Leave H and O until late, especially in acidic solution questions.

Notes

Core definitions

Oxidation
Defn – oxidation is the loss of electrons.
Reduction
Defn – reduction is the gain of electrons.
Memory aid: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

Both oxidation and reduction occur together in a redox reaction. If one species loses electrons, another species gains them.

The species that loses electrons is the reducing agent. The species that gains electrons is the oxidising agent.

Oxidation number

Oxidation number
Defn – the charge an element has or appears to have when it is in a compound when certain rules are applied.

Oxidation and reduction in terms of oxidation number:

Oxidation in oxidation-number terms
Defn – oxidation is an increase in oxidation number.
Reduction in oxidation-number terms
Defn – reduction is a decrease in oxidation number.

Rules for oxidation numbers

  1. Simple elements have oxidation number 0.
  2. Group 1 elements are +1 in compounds; Group 2 elements are +2.
  3. Simple ions have oxidation number equal to charge.
  4. Halogens are usually −1, unless bonded to a more electronegative element.
  5. Oxygen is usually −2, except in OF₂ / O₂F₂ and peroxides.
  6. Hydrogen is usually +1, except in metal hydrides where it is −1.
  7. In complex ions, the oxidation numbers add to the ionic charge.
  8. In neutral molecules, the oxidation numbers add to zero.
Important: Transition-metal oxidation numbers often need you to recognise the attached complex ion first, for example sulfate in FeSO₄.

Worked pattern

A reliable method is: assign oxidation numbers, compare left and right, then state exactly where oxidation and reduction have occurred.

Mg + Cl₂ → MgCl₂
Mg: 0 → +2 (oxidation)    Cl: 0 → −1 (reduction)

This shows why Mg is the reducing agent and chlorine is the oxidising agent.

Balancing redox equations using oxidation numbers

A full worked example is given in acidic solution with permanganate and chloride, using the oxidation-number method rather than half-equations. The intended chemistry is:

2MnO₄⁻ + 16H⁺ + 10Cl⁻ → 2Mn²⁺ + 8H₂O + 5Cl₂

A useful method is: identify Mn(+7) → Mn(+2) as gain of 5e, and Cl(−1) → Cl₂(0) as loss of 1e per Cl, then multiply to equalise electrons.

Exam trap: once you have the electron ratio, go back to the original equation and balance the remaining atoms by inspection. leave H until last.

Social and applied aspects

  • Sodium hypochlorite in bleach oxidises dyes in fabrics.
  • Chlorine disinfects drinking water and swimming pools by oxidising enzymes in microorganisms.
  • Hydrogen peroxide oxidises pigments in hair.
  • Sulphur dioxide acts as a reducing agent in paper bleaching.

Past-paper focus

Practice includes a long run of Leaving Cert questions from 2002 to 2024. The recurring patterns are:

  • Define oxidation / reduction in electron-transfer and oxidation-number terms.
  • Assign oxidation numbers in unusual molecules like OF₂, hydrides, peroxides and complex ions.
  • Identify oxidising and reducing agents.
  • Balance ionic redox equations in acidic solution.

Quick answer outlines

Definitions

Define oxidation and reduction in terms of (i) electron transfer and (ii) oxidation number.

Show answer outline
  • Oxidation: loss of electrons / increase in oxidation number.
  • Reduction: gain of electrons / decrease in oxidation number.
Oxidising and reducing agents

How do you identify the oxidising agent and the reducing agent in a redox equation?

Show answer outline
  • Oxidising agent: the species that is reduced.
  • Reducing agent: the species that is oxidised.
  • Track oxidation numbers or electron transfer to decide.
Manganate(VII)

What colour change is seen when acidified potassium manganate(VII) is reduced fully in solution?

Show answer outline
  • Observation: purple to colourless.
  • Reason: MnO4− is reduced to Mn2+.
Rusting

What is rusting and what conditions are needed for it to occur?

Show answer outline
  • Rusting: corrosion of iron.
  • Conditions: oxygen and water.
  • Result: hydrated iron(III) oxide forms.
Anodising aluminium

How is aluminium anodised?

Show answer outline
  • The aluminium object is made the anode.
  • An electrolyte such as dilute sulfuric acid is used.
  • A thicker protective oxide layer forms on the aluminium surface.
