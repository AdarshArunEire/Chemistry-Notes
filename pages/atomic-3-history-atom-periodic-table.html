History of the Periodic Table

Dobereiner, Johann (1780-1849) noticed that there were groups of three elements with similar properties i.e. calcium, strontium and barium. He also noticed that the atomic weight of one of these elements was midway between the other two. He called these groups of three elements ‘triads’. Other examples are Li, Na and K or Cl, Br and I.

Newlands, John (1837-1898) listed the elements known at the time in order of increasing atomic weight and noticed that every eighth element has similar properties to the first one. This only worked for the first 16 elements but was an important discovery because it showed that the elements had periodic properties. He called this development the ‘Law of Octaves’. This does not apply to today’s periodic table as today we have noble gases and Newlands did not.

Mendeleev, Dimitri (1834-1907) also listed the elements in order of increasing atomic weight. He placed elements with similar properties under each other. He left gaps for undiscovered elements and predicted what these elements would be like i.e. gallium and scandium. He reversed the order of pairs of elements so that they would be listed in groups of similar properties e.g. Te and I.

Moseley, Henry (1887-1915) discovered that the nucleus of each element contained a positive charge of a definite amount. This is the atomic number of each element. (This explained why Mendeleev had to reverse pairs of elements thus elements are now arranged in order of increasing atomic number and why the transition elements are now in a separate group.)

History of the Atom

The Greeks – Most Greek philosophers, including Plato and Aristotle believed that matter was infinitely divisible. Democritus (460-370 B.C.) disagreed and argued that matter is composed of small, indivisible particles, which he called atomos, hence the name atom.

Dalton, John (1766-1844) – an English chemist proposed the following meaningful theory during the period 1803-1807.

  • Matter is made up of small indivisible particles called atoms.
  • All atoms in a given element are identical in mass and properties.
  • These atoms cannot be created or destroyed by chemical reactions.
  • Atoms combine together to form compounds whose properties are different from the atoms from which they were formed.

Although some of Dalton’s ideas have since been shown to be incorrect (i.e. the first two are assumptions), his theory formed the basis for much of the work of chemists in the nineteenth century.

Crookes, William (1832-1919) – an English chemist and physicist. In 1855 he carried out a series of investigations into the behaviour of metals heated in a vacuum.

Electricity was passed through evacuated tubes with two oppositely charged electrodes. A high voltage of electricity was imposed and produced radiation within the tube. Rays of light were observed coming from the negative electrode (cathode) towards the positive electrode (anode). These rays were invisible but they cast a shadow when an object was placed in their path. Crookes, by experiment concluded that these cathode rays were made of particles coming from the cathode. These particles travelled in a straight line but it was found that magnetic and electric fields ‘bent’ the rays in the manner expected for negatively charged particles (electrons). It was also found that cathode rays emitted by different cathode materials were the same.

In the late 19th century scientists debated as to what exactly cathode rays actually were. Some said that they were waves, others said particles. Johnstone Stoney (1826-1911), an Irish physicist believed they were made up of particles and even went so far as to name them ‘electrons’.

Thomson J.J. (1856-1940) – an English professor of physics.

Thomson noticed that when he passed the cathode rays between two oppositely charged plates the beam was attracted towards the positive plate hence the beam must consist of negatively charged particles (electrons). He is credited with the discovery of the first subatomic particle, the electron.

His experiments also allowed him to measure the ratio of the charge of the electron to its mass i.e. its e/m.

Thomson also proposed in 1904 a model of the atom, known as the ‘plum pudding’ model. The atom was a positive sphere with negative electrons embedded in it in a random fashion.

Millikan, Robert (1868-1953) – an American physicist.

“My idea of an educated person is one who can converse on one subject for more than two minutes”

In 1901 he measure the size of the charge of electrons using the ‘oil drop’ experiment. Tiny droplets of oil were sprayed between two charged metal plates. The oil droplets were forced to pick up electrons to become negatively charged. When there is no electric field (voltage) present the drops fall with a steady velocity. He then placed a positive charge on the top plate and saw that the negatively charged oil droplet was attracted to it. The droplet was also falling towards the bottom negatively charged plate due to gravity. The drop’s charge can be found by careful measurement of the voltage needed to bring the drop to rest (suspended) and the rate at which it falls when there is no voltage between the plates.

Rutherford, Ernest (1871-1937) – a New Zealand physicist.

“It was quite the most incredible event that has ever happened to me in my life. It was almost as incredible as it you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you.”

He is famous for his ‘gold foil’ experiment. He bombarded extremely thin gold foil with alpha particles (these are positively charged particles). He was surprised by the results as he expected all of the alpha particles to pass straight through the foil with little or no deflection.

  • (1) Most of the alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil (undeflected), showing that the atoms of gold must be made up of empty space. The alpha particles were detected by hitting off a zinc sulfide screen. Hence zinc sulfide glows where a alpha particle hits.

Chadwick, James (1891-1974) – A past pupil of Rutherford’s. Chadwick bombarded Be foil with alpha particles and neutrally charged particles were emitted from the nucleus. He called these neutral particles neutrons. Chadwick’s work corrected the problem of atomic mass not matching proton number alone.

Show answer outline
  • Cathode rays: streams of negatively charged particles from the cathode.
  • Periodic system: periodic recurrence of chemical properties.
  • Te/I: reversed to keep similar properties together.
  • No noble gases: not yet discovered.
  • Modern table: arranged by atomic number.
