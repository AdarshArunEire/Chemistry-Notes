Limiting reactant idea

Notes

Calculations from balanced equations

A balanced equation gives us a lot of information about the moles of substances that react together (reactants) and the moles of substances formed (products). We can use this information to calculate amounts of reactants used up and to determine the amounts of products produced in the equation, using whatever units they require in the question.

Recognition tip: if the question gives information about one substance and asks you about a different substance, you usually need the balanced equation and the reacting molar ratio.

1st type — you must produce the balanced equation yourself

Question: What mass of magnesium oxide would be produced by the complete combustion of 1.3 grams of magnesium? (LCH 1995)

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
  • Find the moles of Mg: 1.3 ÷ 24 ≈ 0.05 mol.
  • Use the reacting ratio 1:1 between Mg and MgO.
  • So 0.05 mol Mg produces 0.05 mol MgO.
  • Mr(MgO) = 24 + 16 = 40, so mass = 0.05 × 40 = 2 g.
Answer: 2 g of magnesium oxide.

2nd type — the balanced equation is already given

Question: In an experiment, 4.35 g of manganese dioxide reacted with hydrochloric acid according to the balanced equation below. Calculate the number of moles of MnO₂ used, the number of moles of HCl used, the mass of MnCl₂ produced, the volume of chlorine at s.t.p. formed, and the number of chlorine atoms this volume contained.

MnO₂ + 4HCl → MnCl₂ + Cl₂ + 2H₂O
  • Mr(MnO₂) = 55 + 2(16) = 87, so moles of MnO₂ = 4.35 ÷ 87 = 0.05 mol.
  • Use the balanced ratio 1 : 4 : 1 : 1 : 2.
  • HCl used = 4 × 0.05 = 0.20 mol.
  • MnCl₂ produced = 0.05 mol, so mass = 0.05 × 126 = 6.3 g.
  • Cl₂ produced = 0.05 mol, so volume at s.t.p. = 0.05 × 22.4 = 1.12 L.

Volume in questions

You will never be given a volume in a question on its own. You will always be given another piece of information such as s.t.p., r.t.p., concentration (if a solution) or density (if a pure liquid). You must use this information with the volume to calculate the moles of the substance.

  • Molarity (M): moles of solute per litre of solution.
  • % (w/v): grams of solute per 100 cm³ of solution.
  • % (v/v): cm³ of solute per 100 cm³ of solution.
Trap: a volume by itself is not enough. Convert to moles using the extra information the question gives you.

Calculations involving an excess of one reactant

In this type of question you will be given information about two reactants and asked to show one of these is in excess of the other or that one is the limiting reactant.

Suppose you have 10 slices of bread and 7 slices of ham and you want to make sandwiches using one slice of ham and two slices of bread. How many sandwiches could you make? The answer is 5 — before you run out of bread. There will be 2 slices of ham in excess. The bread limits the number of sandwiches (product) made.

A similar situation occurs in chemical reactions when one of the reactants is used up before the other. The reaction stops when this occurs. The reactant that is used up first is called the limiting reactant and the other reactant is said to be in excess. The limiting reactant determines the amount of product produced.

LIMITING REACTANT
Defn – the limiting reactant is the reactant that is fully consumed when a reaction goes to completion.

Worked example — calcium and oxygen

Question: In an experiment 5 g of calcium is reacted with 2.56 g of oxygen according to the given chemical equation. Determine by calculation the limiting reagent in this reaction.

2Ca + O₂ → 2CaO
  • Convert both reactants to moles first.
  • 5 g Ca gives 5 ÷ 40 = 0.125 mol Ca.
  • 2.56 g O₂ gives 2.56 ÷ 32 = 0.08 mol O₂.
  • Use one value and the balanced ratio to find how much of the other reactant is needed.
  • 0.125 mol Ca needs 0.0625 mol O₂.
  • You have 0.08 mol O₂, so oxygen is in excess and calcium is limiting.
Conclusion: calcium is the limiting reactant.

Method to determine a limiting reactant

  1. Convert information given about both reactants into moles to find the amount you “have” of each reactant.
  2. Choose one of these numbers and use it and the molar ratio to find how much of the other reactant you “need”.
  3. Compare the amount you “have” to the amount you “need”.
  4. If you have more than you need, that reactant is in excess. If you need more than you have, that substance is the limiting reactant.

Actual yield, theoretical yield and percentage yield

We now know that the amount of product formed is dependent on the limiting reactant.

In some questions we will be given the amounts of the reactants used and the amount of product formed. The amount of product formed that is given in the question is the actual yield of the product. However this may not be the amount of product that could have been produced according to the balanced chemical equation — this amount is called the theoretical yield.

THEORETICAL YIELD
Defn – the theoretical yield of a reaction is the quantity of product that should form when all of the limiting reactant reacts.
% yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100
Really, really, really important relationship: limiting reactant to product ratio from the balanced equation = theoretical yield.

Worked example — ethanal preparation

A group of students prepared ethanal by the reaction between acidified sodium dichromate(VI) and ethanol. The masses used were 17.88 g of sodium dichromate(VI) and 11.04 g of ethanol. The yield of ethanal was found to be 2.97 g. Show clearly that ethanol is in excess and calculate the percentage yield of ethanal.

  • Mr(Na₂Cr₂O₇·2H₂O) = 298, so moles used = 17.88 ÷ 298 = 0.06 mol.
  • Mr(C₂H₅OH) = 46, so moles used = 11.04 ÷ 46 = 0.24 mol.
  • From the given molar ratio, 0.06 mol dichromate needs 0.18 mol ethanol.
  • We have 0.24 mol ethanol, so ethanol is in excess and dichromate is the limiting reactant.
  • The theoretical yield of ethanal is 0.18 mol.
  • Mr(ethanal) = 44, so theoretical mass = 0.18 × 44 = 7.92 g.
  • Actual yield = 2.97 g.
  • % yield = (2.97 / 7.92) × 100 = 37.5%.
Answer: ethanol is in excess; percentage yield of ethanal = 37.5%.

Percentage yield questions

For ethanal or similar preparation questions, keep theoretical yield and actual yield separate.

Show answer outline
  • Theoretical yield: amount predicted from the balanced equation.
  • Actual yield: amount actually obtained in the lab.
  • Percentage yield: actual ÷ theoretical × 100.
  • If percentage yield is given, rearrange to find the actual mass.
