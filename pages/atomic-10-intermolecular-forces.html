Atomic Theory 10 — Intermolecular Forces

At a glance

This chapter explains intermolecular forces: van der Waals forces, dipole–dipole attractions and hydrogen bonding, then links them to boiling point, state of matter and solubility.

All molecules: van der Waals Polar molecules: dipole–dipole N–H / O–H / F–H: hydrogen bonding

Intermolecular forces of attraction

Intermolecular forces – forces of attraction between molecules.

These are much weaker than intramolecular bonds, but they strongly affect boiling point, melting point, solubility and state of matter.

ForceWhere it occursRelative strengthMain effect
Van der Waalsbetween all moleculesweakestimportant in non-polar substances
Dipole–dipolebetween polar moleculesstrongerraises b.p. above similar non-polar molecules
Hydrogen bondingH bonded to N, O or Fstrongest molecular force herevery high b.p., unusual solubility effects

Hydrogen bonding

Hydrogen bond – a strong dipole–dipole attraction between a hydrogen atom bonded to a very electronegative atom (N, O or F) and a lone pair on N, O or F in a nearby molecule.

The notes emphasise that hydrogen bonding is relatively weak compared with covalent bonds, but strong enough to have a large effect on physical properties.

  • Water has an unusually high boiling point because of hydrogen bonding.
  • Water is liquid at room temperature while H₂S is a gas because water molecules are much more strongly attracted to each other.
  • Hydrogen bonding also helps explain the high solubility of many polar substances in water.

Hydrogen bonding between water molecules

Van der Waals forces and dipole–dipole attractions

Van der Waals forces – weak intermolecular attractions caused by temporary shifts in electron distribution.
Dipole–dipole attractions – attractions between permanent dipoles in polar molecules.

Van der Waals forces exist between all substances. Dipole–dipole attractions occur in polar substances and are stronger than van der Waals forces alone.

Boiling points and states of matter

The boiling point and state of matter depend on two linked ideas:

  • the strength of the intermolecular forces, and
  • the size / mass of the molecules.
Remember: everything is relative. Larger molecules usually have stronger intermolecular attractions than smaller similar molecules.

Non-polar covalentvan der Waalsvery low b.p.; often gases

Practice prompts

Leaving Cert style

Account for the higher boiling point of oxygen than hydrogen.

Show answer

Answer: oxygen molecules are larger and have more electrons, so they have stronger van der Waals forces than hydrogen molecules. More energy is needed to separate them.

Leaving Cert style

Explain the term intermolecular forces.

Show answer

Answer: intermolecular forces are forces of attraction between molecules.

Leaving Cert style

Why does methane have a very low boiling point?

Show answer
  • Methane is non-polar.
  • Only van der Waals forces act between methane molecules.
  • These forces are very weak, so little energy is needed to separate the molecules.

Leaving Cert style

Why is the boiling point of water far higher than that of methane, even though their relative molecular masses are similar?

Show answer
  • Water molecules hydrogen-bond to each other.
  • Methane molecules only have van der Waals forces.
  • Hydrogen bonding is much stronger, so far more energy is needed to boil water.

Leaving Cert style

Which of NH₃ and SiH₄ has hydrogen bonding between its molecules? Justify your answer.

Show answer

NH₃ has hydrogen bonding because hydrogen is bonded to nitrogen, which is small and highly electronegative. SiH₄ does not.
