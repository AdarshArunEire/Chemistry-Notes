============================================================ PLANAR CARBON

PLANAR CARBON

Planar Carbon – It may have one double bond and two single bonds OR one triple bond and one single bond. All of the following homologous series will contain at least one planar carbon in their structure.

All angles around a planar carbon are 120° (trigonal planar geometry). Compare this to a tetrahedral carbon (4 single bonds, 109.5°).

Tetrahedral Carbon (sp³)
Planar Carbon (sp²) – e.g. aldehyde C

Alkenes and Alkynes

As in Introduction to Organic Chemistry handout 1 – please revise. Both of these contain two planar carbons in their structure.

============================================================ ALDEHYDES

ALDEHYDES

  1. Aldehydes are a homologous series containing the –CHO functional group:
    Aldehyde functional group –CHO  (planar carbon)
  2. Aldehydes contain a carbonyl group (C=O). This carbonyl group is polar due to the difference in electronegativity values of the carbon and oxygen atoms. The oxygen is δ− (partially negative) as it is more electronegative and the carbon is δ+ (partially positive) as it is less electronegative.
    Polar C=O bond in aldehyde – dipole-dipole attractions result
  3. As a result of the carbonyl group there will be dipole-dipole attractions between the aldehyde molecules making them have higher boiling points than corresponding alkane molecules BUT lower boiling points than corresponding alcohol molecules (alcohols have –OH which allows full hydrogen bonding between molecules, whereas aldehydes cannot hydrogen bond with each other).
  4. The lower aldehydes are soluble in both water and non-polar solvents such as cyclohexane. The reason why the lower aldehydes are soluble in water, a polar solvent, is due to hydrogen bonding that occurs between the oxygen of the carbonyl group of the aldehyde and the hydrogen of water. The higher aldehydes are insoluble in water as the non-polar carbon chain of the aldehyde molecule has greater influence.
    Hydrogen bonding between aldehyde (lone pair on O) and water (H)
  5. When naming aldehydes the ending will be –anal i.e. methanal, ethanal, propanal etc.
  6. N.B. Methanal is a gas, while ethanal is a volatile liquid at room temperature.
  7. The functional group is always written at the right of the molecule and the carbon of the functional group is always carbon 1 when naming. The usual rules for naming apply.

============================================================ KETONES

KETONES

  1. Ketones have the general formula R–CO–R', where R and R' are alkyl groups which may be the same or different. (R and R' cannot be hydrogen!!!)
    Ketone: C=O group flanked by two alkyl groups
  2. Ketones also contain the carbonyl group as their functional group, –C=O and so the physical properties show a lot of similarities to aldehydes.
  3. The boiling points of ketones are higher than that of corresponding alkanes due to the dipole-dipole attractions between the polar carbonyl groups but again lower than corresponding alcohols.
  4. The lower ketones are soluble in polar water due to hydrogen bonding between the oxygen of the carbonyl group of the ketone and the hydrogen in water. Like the alcohols and aldehydes, the solubility in water decreases as the length of the non-polar carbon chain increases. Ketones are soluble in non-polar solvents like cyclohexane.
  5. Propanone is an excellent non-polar solvent itself. It is found in nail varnish remover (old name is acetone).
  6. When naming ketones the ending –anone is used i.e. propanone, butanone etc.
  7. The carbon of the functional group is never the first or last carbon of the molecule. Its position is indicated by the smallest possible number and then the usual rules apply when naming.
  8. The first member of the homologous series is propanone as ketones can only have a minimum of 3 carbons. There is no need to use the number in the name for propanone and butanone, as there is only one possible location for the functional group. However, pentanone has pentan-2-one and pentan-3-one.

============================================================ CARBOXYLIC ACIDS

CARBOXYLIC ACIDS

  1. Carboxylic acids have a general formula of R–COOH, where R is either hydrogen or an alkyl group.
  2. The functional group is –COOH (the carboxyl group):
    –COOH functional group: carbonyl C=O + hydroxyl –OH combined
  3. The boiling points of carboxylic acids is considerably higher than the corresponding alcohols due to the hydrogen bonding present between the molecules caused by the polar –OH group present in the functional group. The molecules also group together in twos (called dimers) held together by hydrogen bonds. As a result, more energy is needed to break these hydrogen bonds, hence the highest boiling points of all the homologous series.
    very high b.p.
    Carboxylic acid dimer: two molecules linked by two hydrogen bonds
  4. The carboxylic acids are liquids at room temperature also due to the hydrogen bonds holding the molecules together.
  5. The lower carboxylic acids are soluble in polar water due to the formation of hydrogen bonds with water. The oxygen of the carbonyl group of the acid forms a hydrogen bond with the hydrogen in water and the hydrogen of the –OH group of the acid forms another hydrogen bond with the oxygen of water. As the length of the carbon chain increases (non-polar part) increases, solubility in water decreases. The higher members of the acids are soluble in non-polar solvents such as cyclohexane.
  6. When naming carboxylic acids the functional group is always on the right hand side of the molecule hence is carbon 1 and if the molecule has groups attached they must be named from this end. The endings are –anoic acid i.e. methanoic acid, ethanoic acid, propanoic acid etc.
  7. Methanoic acid (formic acid) is found in nettle and ant stings.
    Ethanoic acid is used in the manufacture of cellulose acetate, which is used in varnishes and is also found in vinegar.
    Propanoic acid and benzoic acid (aromatic) are used as food preservatives.
  8. Methanoic acid is unusual as it also can act like aldehydes as its structure also contains the aldehyde functional group. This will be discussed when examining reactions of the carboxylic acids!

============================================================ ESTERS

ESTERS

  1. Esters have a general formula of R–COO–R', where R is either hydrogen or an alkyl group and R' is an alkyl group. The functional group of the esters is –COO– (the ester linkage, circled):
    Ester group –COO–: the C=O part comes from the acid; the –O– part comes from the alcohol
  2. Esters are compounds formed when the hydrogen of the functional group of a carboxylic acid is replaced by an alkyl radical of an alcohol.
  3. They are formed by the reversible reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol. This is known as a CONDENSATION REACTION:
    Condensation Reaction: A condensation reaction occurs when two different molecules combine to form a more complex molecule with the production of a smaller molecule such as water (H₂O).
  4. They have relatively low boiling points due to dipole-dipole attractions between the molecules (no H-bonding between ester molecules themselves).
  5. The lower esters are soluble in polar water as the oxygen of the carbonyl group forms hydrogen bonds with the hydrogen of water. Solubility decreases as the length of the carbon chain increases. The higher esters are soluble in non-polar solvents such as cyclohexane.

Naming Esters

Rule: The part of the ester with the carbon of the functional group comes from the carboxylic acid. The end part comes from the alcohol.
Esters are named 'backwards'. The alkyl group at the end of the molecule comes first in the name with a '-yl' ending. The front part of the molecule is then given an '–anoate' ending.
ethyl front = ethanoate → ethyl ethanoate
propyl front = methanoate → propyl methanoate

Polyesters

Polyesters are a term you should be familiar with. It is a name given to synthetic fabrics such as 'terylene' for example. Used in the clothing industry and other fabric industries (carpet, sails etc.).

Polyesters are polymers that consist of hundreds of thousands of ester molecules linked together under certain conditions of temperature and pressure.

Fats and Oils

Fats and oils are esters that occur naturally. An example of one such ester is Glyceryl Tristearate, which is the ester used to make soap in our mandatory experiment (handout 8).

Glyceryl tristearate: glycerol + 3 stearic acid chains joined by ester bonds (–O–CO–)

Perfumes

Esters have distinct aromas (smells) and are responsible for the smells that come from petals of flowers and fruits. Synthetic esters are manufactured in the lab to imitate these smells and are used in perfumes and soaps and bath salts and deodorants and such like 'smelly' products.

Solvents

Ethyl ethanoate is used as a non-polar solvent.

============================================================ REDOX REACTIONS

REDOX REACTIONS

The preparation of an aldehyde, ketone and a carboxylic acid are redox reactions as the alcohol is oxidised and the oxidising agent (sodium dichromate) is reduced during the preparation. The reduction of the dichromate ion during these reactions leads to a colour change of orange (Cr₂O₇²⁻) to green (Cr³⁺).

Primary alcohol → Aldehyde → Carboxylic acid  |  Secondary alcohol → Ketone (stops here)
============================================================ DIAGNOSTIC TESTS

DIAGNOSTIC TESTS – REACTIONS OF ETHANAL

Ethanal is an excellent reducing agent. These tests detect the presence of aldehydes. (Q2 – know these!)

1. Reaction with Acidified Potassium Permanganate (KMnO₄)

  • Place a few cm³ of ethanal into a test tube with some acidified potassium manganate(VII) (KMnO₄).
  • Heat gently in a water bath for a few minutes.
Result: The purple colour of the manganate(VII) goes colourless as the ethanal is a reducing agent and reduces MnO₄⁻ (purple) to Mn²⁺ (colourless).
MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O
Purple → Colourless
Q: Why should the manganate(VII) solution be acidified?
A: The H⁺ ions (from H₂SO₄) are needed as a reactant in the half-equation — they are consumed in the reduction. Without acid, the reaction does not proceed properly.

2. Reaction with Fehling's Solution

  • Place a few cm³ of ethanal into a test tube.
  • Add equal volumes of Fehling's solution A + B (blue in colour due to presence of Cu²⁺).
  • Heat gently and shake to mix.
Result: The blue colour of the Fehling's solution changes to a red precipitate. This is due to ethanal acting as a reducing agent which reduces Cu²⁺ (blue) to Cu₂O (red ppt).
2Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → 2Cu⁺ (as Cu₂O – red ppt)
Blue (Cu²⁺) → Red precipitate (Cu₂O)
Note: Ethanal is a reducing agent so it gets oxidised during this reaction to ethanoic acid.

3. Reaction with Tollen's Reagent – "The Silver Mirror Test"

  • Place a few cm³ of silver nitrate solution into a test tube. Add a small amount of NaOH.
  • Add a few drops of ammonia (aqueous) until the silver oxide precipitate disappears.
  • Add a few drops of ethanal and warm gently.
Result: A deposit of metallic silver is seen on the inside of the test tube (silver mirror). This is due to the silver in Tollen's Reagent (Ag⁺, oxidation number +1) being reduced by ethanal to metallic silver (Ag⁰, oxidation number 0).
Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag⁰    (Ag⁺ is reduced to silver metal)
Silver mirror forms on glass

Preparation of a Ketone – Note

To produce a ketone, we use a secondary alcohol (e.g. propan-2-ol → propanone). As ketones are not easily oxidised on to become the carboxylic acid, it is not necessary to use the same precautions as for the aldehyde preparation (i.e. excess alcohol over oxidising agent is not required).
============================================================ REDUCTION

REDUCTION OF ALDEHYDES AND KETONES

  1. Aldehydes are reduced to the primary alcohol:
    • Reagent: Hydrogen (H₂)
    • Condition: Nickel catalyst (Ni)
    Ethanal + H₂ → Ethanol
    CH₃CHO + H₂ [Ni catalyst] → CH₃CH₂OH
    Aldehyde → Primary alcohol
  2. Ketones are reduced to the secondary alcohol:
    • Reagent: Hydrogen (H₂)
    • Condition: Nickel catalyst (Ni)
    Propanone + H₂ → Propan-2-ol
    CH₃COCH₃ + H₂ [Ni catalyst] → CH₃CH(OH)CH₃
    Ketone → Secondary alcohol
============================================================ REACTIONS OF ETHANOIC ACID

REACTIONS OF ETHANOIC ACID

1. Reaction with Sodium Carbonate – Diagnostic Test for Carboxylic Acids (Q2)

  • A spatula load of sodium carbonate was placed into a test tube.
  • A few cm³ of ethanoic acid was added.
Result: Effervescence was observed as carbon dioxide was given off. This carbon dioxide quenched a lighted taper held at the mouth of the test tube.
2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂↑
Ethanoic acid + Sodium carbonate → Sodium ethanoate + Water + Carbon dioxide

2. Reaction with Magnesium Metal

  • A test tube was quarter filled with ethanoic acid.
  • About 3 cm of magnesium ribbon was added.
Result: The magnesium ribbon disappeared and hydrogen gas was given off.
2CH₃COOH + Mg → (CH₃COO)₂Mg + H₂↑
Ethanoic acid + Magnesium → Magnesium ethanoate + Hydrogen

3. Esterification – Condensation Reaction with Ethanol

  • When a carboxylic acid and an alcohol are reacted together under reflux in the presence of concentrated sulfuric acid, an ester is formed.
  • This reaction is referred to as an esterification reaction. It may also be called a substitution reaction (the H of the –OH of the acid is replaced by an alkyl group from an alcohol). It is also a condensation reaction (two molecules form a more complex molecule + water).
  • When ethanoic acid and ethanol are refluxed together the ester ethyl ethanoate is formed.
CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O
Ethanoic acid + Ethanol ⇌ Ethyl ethanoate + Water  [conc. H₂SO₄, reflux]

This is a reversible reaction and so is affected by changes of concentration and temperature (but not pressure — because all reactants and products are liquids/dissolved species, so pressure has no effect on equilibrium position).

Two purposes of concentrated sulfuric acid:
  1. Dehydrating agent — removes water produced, decreasing [H₂O], which shifts the equilibrium to the right (forward reaction favoured, more ester produced).
  2. Catalyst — speeds up the rate of the reaction.
Reflux Diagram

Reflux Apparatus (★ Draw and Label – Exam Question)

What happens during reflux: The liquid in the flask boils. Vapours rise up into the condenser, cool, condense back to liquid, and drip back into the flask — preventing loss of volatile reactants and allowing more time for the reaction to proceed to completion.

Reduction of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids are reduced to firstly an aldehyde then to a primary alcohol using hydrogen and a nickel catalyst.

RCOOH + 2H₂ [Ni catalyst] → RCHO + H₂O → RCH₂OH
Carboxylic acid → Aldehyde → Primary alcohol

Leaving Cert 2006

The chart compares the boiling points of alkanes and primary alcohols containing from one to four carbon atoms.

  1. Give two reasons why each of these alcohols has a higher boiling point than the corresponding alkane. (7)
    (1) Alcohols can form hydrogen bonds between molecules due to the polar –OH group; alkanes only have weak van der Waals forces.
    (2) Hydrogen bonds are stronger than van der Waals forces, so more energy is required to separate alcohol molecules.
  2. Explain why the difference in boiling points between methane and methanol is 226.5 K while the difference in boiling points between butane and butanol is only 119 K. (6)
    As the carbon chain length increases, the van der Waals forces in the alkane become stronger and contribute more to the boiling point. The H-bonding in the alcohol contributes the same, but the relative effect of H-bonding decreases as the alkane b.p. rises. So the difference (alcohol − alkane) gets smaller.
  3. Describe, in general terms, the solubilities of methane and methanol, butane and butanol in water. (12)
    Methane: insoluble in water (non-polar, no H-bonding). Methanol: miscible with water (polar –OH, H-bonds with water). Butane: insoluble (long non-polar chain). Butanol: slightly soluble — the –OH still allows some H-bonding but the long non-polar chain reduces solubility significantly.
Show answer outline
  • Reason 1: alcohols form hydrogen bonds.
  • Reason 2: hydrogen bonding is stronger than the van der Waals forces in alkanes.
