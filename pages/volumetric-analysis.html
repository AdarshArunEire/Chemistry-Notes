Notes

What volumetric analysis is

Volumetric analysis involves reacting two solutions together to find the concentration of one of them. To know the concentration of a solution you need two pieces of information: the amount of solute and the amount of solution. Only the solutes react with each other; the solvent does not react.

A solution contains a solute and a solvent. In the experiments in this chapter the solvent is water. The solute may be a solid, such as sodium carbonate, sodium thiosulfate crystals, or iron tablets, or a liquid, such as vinegar or bleach.

Concentration of a solution – amount of solute per amount of solution.

If there is a high proportion of solute to solvent the solution is concentrated. If there is a low proportion of solute to solvent the solution is dilute.

Expressing concentration

% weight per volume, % (w/v)

Grams of solute per 100 cm³ of solution. A 10% (w/v) potassium iodide solution contains 10 g KI per 100 cm³ of solution.

% weight per weight, % (w/w)

Grams of solute per 100 g of solution. A 10% (w/w) sodium chloride solution contains 10 g NaCl per 100 g of solution.

% volume per volume, % (v/v)

cm³ of solute per 100 cm³ of solution. A 12% (v/v) ethanol solution contains 12 cm³ ethanol per 100 cm³ of solution.

Applied example: alcohol content in drinks is commonly given as % (v/v). Beer is about 4% (v/v), while spirits such as gin or vodka are often about 40% (v/v).

Parts per million, p.p.m.

Used for very small concentrations. In this course, 1 p.p.m. = 1 mg L−1.

Molarity

The usual unit is moles per litre. A 1 M solution contains 1 mole of solute made up to 1 litre of solution.

Examples: 1 M NaOH contains 40 g NaOH per litre. 0.5 M NaOH contains 20 g NaOH per litre.

Dilution

Stock solutions are concentrated solutions kept for storage and later use. On dilution, water is added, so the concentration changes but the moles of solute do not.

Key point: moles of solute after dilution = moles of solute before dilution.
C₁V₁ = C₂V₂

Colour intensity and colorimetry

Strongly coloured solutions become paler as they are diluted. Potassium manganate(VII) is deep purple due to Mn(+7), and hydrated copper(II) sulfate gives a vivid blue solution due to Cu(+2). Colour intensity can be measured with a colorimeter. The technique is called colorimetry.

Standard solutions, primary standards and secondary standards

Standard solution – a solution of known concentration.

There is always one standard solution in a titration: the one whose concentration is already known.

Primary standard – a solute that can be made into a standard solution directly because it is available in 100% purity, is stable in air, has a high molar mass for accurate weighing, and dissolves readily in water.

Most standard solutions are made from primary standards. A carefully weighed mass is made up to a known volume in a volumetric flask, so the concentration can be calculated directly without titration.

Primary standards on this syllabus: anhydrous sodium carbonate, Na2CO3, and ammonium iron(II) sulfate, (NH4)2SO4·FeSO4·6H2O.
Secondary standard – a solute that cannot be made into a standard solution directly because it is not available in 100% purity, is not stable in air, has a low molar mass, or does not dissolve readily in water.
Secondary standards on this syllabus:
  • NaOH – deliquescent, so it absorbs moisture from the air.
  • KMnO4 – decomposes in strong sunlight, is easily reduced, and does not dissolve readily in water.
  • I2 – sublimes, so it is not stable in air.
  • Na2S2O3 – not available in 100% purity.
To standardise – to find the concentration of a solution by titration or colorimetry.

General practical procedures

Preparing a solution in a volumetric flask

  1. Weigh the solid accurately on a clock glass using an electronic balance.
  2. Transfer all of the solute to a beaker and dissolve it using deionised water and a clean glass rod.
  3. Pour the solution into the volumetric flask through a funnel.
  4. Wash the beaker, funnel and rod with deionised water and add the rinsings to the flask.
  5. Remove the funnel and add deionised water until the level is about 1 cm below the mark.
  6. Add deionised water drop by drop until the bottom of the meniscus is on the graduation mark at eye level.
  7. Stopper and invert 20–30 times so the solution becomes homogeneous.
Exam wording: always say stopper and invert 20–30 times. “Shake” on its own is not accepted.
Liquid solute: if the solute is a liquid, such as vinegar or bleach, measure it with a properly rinsed pipette and transfer it to the volumetric flask before making up to the mark.

Pipette

  • Used at room temperature only.
  • Wash with deionised water, then rinse with the solution it is to contain to prevent dilution.
  • Draw liquid in with a pipette filler until above the mark.
  • Run off until the bottom of the meniscus is on the mark at eye level.
  • Deliver into the conical flask by touching the tip against the inside of the flask.
  • Do not blow out the last drop; it is allowed for in calibration.

Conical flask

  • Its narrow neck reduces spillage during swirling.
  • Rinse only with deionised water, not with the solution to be titrated.
  • Swirl continuously during the titration.
  • Drops on the sides can be washed down with deionised water without affecting the result because dilution does not change the reacting moles already present.
  • Stand the flask on a white tile to make the colour change easier to see.

Burette

  • Used at room temperature and reads to 0.1 cm³.
  • Clamp vertically to avoid parallax error.
  • Rinse with deionised water, then with the solution it is to contain.
  • Fill above the 0 cm³ mark with the tap closed, then remove the funnel.
  • Open the tap briefly so the jet below the tap fills completely.
  • Read from the bottom of the meniscus, except for KMnO4, which is read from the top because it is deeply coloured.
Usual acid–base setup: the acid is usually placed in the burette because bases can clog the tap. If a more accurate colour change is obtained the other way around, the setup may be reversed.

Aim of a titration

The aim is to find the concentration of one solution. To do that you need:

  • the volume of the pipetted solution,
  • the average titre from the burette,
  • the concentration of one of the solutions,
  • and the reacting ratio from the balanced equation.

Experiment 1 — Preparing a standard solution of sodium carbonate

This is not a titration. It is the preparation of a standard solution for use in later titrations.

Result: 2.65 g Na2CO3 made up to 250 cm³ gives a 0.1 M solution.
Calculation: Mr(Na2CO3) = 106, so 2.65 ÷ 106 = 0.025 mol in 250 cm³ = 0.1 mol L−1.

Experiment 2 — Standardisation of HCl using standard Na2CO3

Indicator: methyl orange. Yellow in alkaline solution, pink at the end-point.
2HCl + Na2CO3 → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2

Typical results

TitrationTitre (cm³)
1st (rough)19.9
2nd19.6
3rd19.5
Average19.55

Calculation

For HCl: V1 = 19.55 cm³, M1 = x, n1 = 2

For Na2CO3: V2 = 25.0 cm³, M2 = 0.1 M, n2 = 1

  1. Moles of Na2CO3 = 0.1 × 25 / 1000 = 0.0025 mol
  2. Moles of HCl = 0.0025 × 2 = 0.0050 mol
  3. Molarity of HCl = 0.0050 / 0.01955 = 0.256 M
  4. Grams per litre = 0.256 × 36.5 = 9.344 g L−1
Why use only a few drops of indicator? Indicators are themselves weak acids or weak bases and can take part in the titration. The smallest amount possible is used to minimise their effect.

Experiment 3 — Water of crystallisation in Na2CO3·xH2O

The standardised HCl from Experiment 2 is used to determine the percentage water of crystallisation and the value of x in hydrated sodium carbonate crystals.

Method: find the molarity first, convert that to grams of anhydrous Na2CO3, subtract from the mass of hydrated crystals to get the mass of water, then calculate the percentage and the value of x.
2HCl + Na2CO3 → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2

Worked example

4.30 g of washing soda crystals, Na2CO3·xH2O, were made up to 250 cm³. A 25 cm³ portion required 15 cm³ of 0.2 M HCl.

  1. Moles of HCl = 0.2 × 15/1000 = 0.0030 mol
  2. Moles of Na2CO3 = 0.0030 / 2 = 0.0015 mol
  3. Molarity of Na2CO3 = 0.0015 / 0.025 = 0.06 M
  4. Grams per litre of anhydrous Na2CO3 = 0.06 × 106 = 6.36 g L−1
  5. Only 250 cm³ was made, so mass of anhydrous Na2CO3 present = 6.36 ÷ 4 = 1.59 g
  6. Mass of water of crystallisation = 4.30 − 1.59 = 2.71 g
  7. % water of crystallisation = (2.71 / 4.30) × 100 = 63.02%
  8. x = (2.71 / 18) ÷ (1.59 / 106) = 10

Experiment 4 — Determination of ethanoic acid in vinegar

Weak acid–strong base titration: phenolphthalein is used. The end-point is colourless → pink.
CH3COOH + NaOH → CH3COONa + H2O

25 cm³ of vinegar are diluted to 250 cm³ before titration.

Why was the vinegar diluted? The original vinegar was too concentrated and would require an excessive amount of sodium hydroxide.
Unusual setup: the vinegar is placed in the conical flask and NaOH in the burette so the easier colour change, colourless to pink, is observed at the end-point.
TitrationTitre (cm³)
1st (rough)27.5
2nd27.6
3rd27.5
Average27.55

Using 25.0 cm³ of 0.1 M NaOH:

  1. Moles of NaOH in 25.0 cm³ = 0.1 × 25/1000 = 0.0025 mol
  2. Moles of CH3COOH in 27.55 cm³ of diluted vinegar = 0.0025 mol
  3. Molarity of diluted vinegar = 0.0025 / 0.02755 = 0.0907 M
  4. Molarity of original vinegar = 0.0907 × 10 = 0.907 M
  5. As % (w/v): 0.907 × 60 = 54.4 g L−1 = 5.44% (w/v)

Experiment 5 — Standardisation of KMnO4

This is a redox titration using ammonium iron(II) sulfate as the primary standard.

Ammonium iron(II) sulfate, (NH4)2SO4·FeSO4·6H2O

  • Primary standard.
  • Pipetted into the conical flask.
  • Fe2+ is easily oxidised by air, so dilute sulfuric acid is added during preparation to prevent aerial oxidation.
  • Its high molar mass improves weighing accuracy.

Potassium manganate(VII), KMnO4

  • Oxidising agent, so it is itself reduced.
  • Secondary standard.
  • Deep purple because of Mn(+7).
  • Always placed in the burette.
  • The titration is carried out in acidic conditions so Mn(+7) is reduced fully to Mn(+2).
  • Mn(+2) acts as an autocatalyst.
MnO4− + 5Fe2+ + 8H+ → Mn2+ + 5Fe3+ + 4H2O
End-point: no indicator is needed. The end-point is the first permanent pink tinge.
If insufficient acid is present: Mn(+7) is reduced only to Mn(+4), giving a brown precipitate of MnO2. Add more dilute sulfuric acid.
Do not use HCl or HNO3 to acidify: HCl is oxidised by manganate(VII), while HNO3 is itself an oxidising agent.
Important: the standardised KMnO4 solution should be used immediately in the next titration because its concentration changes on standing.

Experiment 6 — Iron in iron tablets

This uses the same chemistry as Experiment 5, but now the Fe2+ comes from iron tablets.

MnO4− + 5Fe2+ + 8H+ → Mn2+ + 5Fe3+ + 4H2O
  • Five tablets are weighed to reduce percentage error and to provide enough Fe2+ for a measurable titre.
  • The tablets are crushed with dilute sulfuric acid to speed up dissolving and prevent aerial oxidation of Fe2+.
  • More dilute sulfuric acid is added to the conical flask so Mn(+7) is fully reduced to Mn(+2).
TitrationTitre (cm³)
1st (rough)6.2
2nd6.3
3rd6.3
Average6.3

Using 0.02 M KMnO4:

  1. Moles of KMnO4 = 0.02 × 6.3/1000 = 1.26 × 10−4 mol
  2. Moles of Fe2+ = 5 × 1.26 × 10−4 = 6.30 × 10−4 mol
  3. Molarity of Fe2+ solution = 6.30 × 10−4 / 0.025 = 0.0252 M
Read carefully in exam questions: sometimes you are asked for iron, sometimes for iron(II) sulfate.

Experiment 7 — Sodium thiosulfate and iodine titrations

Sodium thiosulfate, Na2S2O3

  • Reducing agent.
  • Secondary standard.
  • Placed in the burette.

Iodine, I2

  • Non-polar and almost insoluble in water.
  • Secondary standard because it sublimes and is not stable in air.
  • Kept in aqueous solution by using excess potassium iodide.
I2 + 2S2O32− → 2I− + S4O62−

Iodometric end-point

  1. The iodine solution in the conical flask starts red/brown.
  2. Add sodium thiosulfate until the solution becomes straw-yellow.
  3. Add a few drops of starch; the solution turns blue-black.
  4. Continue the titration until the solution becomes colourless.
Colour sequence: red/brown → straw-yellow → blue-black → colourless.
Why is starch added near the end? If added too early, the iodine–starch complex is too concentrated and too stable, making the end-point less accurate.

Experiment 8 — Percentage (w/v) of hypochlorite in bleach

Bleach contains hypochlorite ions, ClO−, usually in the form of NaClO. The hypochlorite ion is an oxidising agent and liberates iodine from excess iodide in a 1:1 ratio. The iodine is then titrated with standard sodium thiosulfate.

ClO− + 2I− + 2H+ → Cl− + I2 + H2O
I2 + 2S2O32− → 2I− + S4O62−
Why is the bleach diluted? Original bleach is too concentrated and would require an excessive amount of sodium thiosulfate.
Why must KI be in excess? To ensure all of the oxidising agent is reduced and the maximum amount of iodine is liberated, and to keep the iodine in aqueous solution.

Water titration 1 — Total hardness in a water sample using EDTA

Hardness in water is caused mainly by calcium and magnesium ions from hydrogencarbonates or sulfates. In this titration the hardness is expressed, for simplicity, as if it were all due to CaCO3.

M2+ + H2Y2− → MY2− + 2H+

Here M2+ represents the ions causing hardness, and H2Y2− represents the EDTA reagent. EDTA reacts in a 1:1 ratio with the hardness ions.

  • Indicator: Eriochrome Black T (or Solochrome Black T).
  • Colour change: wine-red → blue.
  • Special condition: add a buffer of pH 10 because the end-point is difficult to detect unless the pH is controlled.
  • Storage note: EDTA is kept in plastic because it can extract metal ions from glass over time.
TitrationTitre (cm³)
1st (rough)15.4
2nd15.2
3rd15.3
Average15.25

Using 25.0 cm³ of water sample and 0.01 M EDTA:

  1. Moles of EDTA = 0.01 × 15.25/1000 = 1.525 × 10−4 mol
  2. Moles of hardness, as CaCO3, in 25.0 cm³ = 1.525 × 10−4 mol
  3. Molarity = 1.525 × 10−4 / 0.025 = 0.00610 M
  4. g L−1 as CaCO3 = 0.00610 × 100 = 0.610 g L−1
  5. ppm = 610 mg L−1 = 610 p.p.m.
Total hardness: 610 p.p.m. as CaCO3.

Water titration 2 — Dissolved oxygen using the Winkler method

This is a redox titration used to determine dissolved oxygen in water.

  1. Add concentrated manganese(II) sulfate solution and alkaline potassium iodide deep into a full bottle of water sample.
  2. A white precipitate of manganese(II) hydroxide forms first and rapidly turns brown in the presence of dissolved oxygen.
  3. Add concentrated sulfuric acid to dissolve the brown precipitate and liberate iodine.
  4. Titrate the iodine with standard sodium thiosulfate using starch near the end-point.
I2 + 2S2O32− → 2I− + S4O62−
Key ratio: 1 mole O2 produces 2 moles I2.
Indicator timing: add starch only when the solution has become straw-yellow.
Colour sequence: brown precipitate → red/brown iodine solution → straw-yellow → blue-black after starch → colourless at the end-point.
TitrationTitre (cm³)
1st (rough)5.9
2nd5.7
3rd5.6
Average5.65

Using 100 cm³ of water sample and 0.02 M sodium thiosulfate:

  1. Moles of thiosulfate = 0.02 × 5.65/1000 = 1.13 × 10−4 mol
  2. Moles of I2 = 1.13 × 10−4 / 2 = 5.65 × 10−5 mol
  3. Moles of O2 = 5.65 × 10−5 / 2 = 2.825 × 10−5 mol in 100 cm³
  4. Moles per litre = 2.825 × 10−4 mol L−1
  5. Mass concentration = 2.825 × 10−4 × 32 = 0.00904 g L−1
  6. Dissolved oxygen = 9.04 p.p.m.
Dissolved oxygen content: 9.04 p.p.m.
Important: the Winkler method does not work for chlorinated water, such as tap water, because chlorine is an oxidising agent and would liberate extra iodine, giving a falsely high result.

Final checklist

  • Know the concentration units and be able to convert between them.
  • Know why each piece of apparatus is rinsed the way it is.
  • Learn the indicators and colour changes for each titration.
  • Know the balanced equations and reacting ratios.
  • Do not forget the two Water titrations: EDTA hardness and Winkler dissolved oxygen.
