Volumetric Analysis

Volumetric Analysis

At a glance

Core titration logic

The key pattern uses the same four inputs: known concentration, two measured volumes, a balanced equation, and an average titre. This topic is a definite question in section A of the exam (Q1).

Notes

What volumetric analysis is

Volumetric analysis involves reacting two solutions together to find the concentration of one of them. To know the concentration of a solution you need two pieces of information (1) the amount of solute and (2) the amount of solution. Only the solutes of each solution react with each other — the solvent does not react.

A solution is made up of two parts, a solute and a solvent. In all of the experiments in this part of the course the solvent is water. The solute is either in solid form (e.g. sodium carbonate, iron tablets, sodium thiosulfate crystals) or in liquid form (e.g. vinegar, bleach).

Concentration of a solution – amount of solute per amount of solution.

If there is a high proportion of solute to solvent the solution will be concentrated. If there is a low proportion of solute to solvent then the solution is said to be dilute.

Expressing concentrations

% Weight per volume (% w/v)

Grams per 100 cm³. A solution of potassium iodide labelled 10% (w/v) means that there is 10 grams of KI per 100 cm³ of solution. To make this solution you would weigh out 10g of KI and add water until the volume of the solution is 100 cm³.

% Weight per weight (% w/w)

Grams per 100 grams. A solution labelled 10% (w/w) of sodium chloride means that there are 10 grams of NaCl per 100 grams of solution.

% Volume per volume (% v/v)

cm³ per 100 cm³. A solution of ethanol labelled 12% (v/v) means that 12 cm³ ethanol was measured out and water was added until the total volume of the solution is 100 cm³.

Social and applied aspect: the amount of alcohol in alcoholic drinks is often given as % (v/v), i.e. beer is about 4% (v/v) whereas gin or vodka usually have an alcohol content of about 40% (v/v).

Parts per million (ppm)

These units are usually used to describe very small concentrations. 1 ppm = 1 mg per litre. This unit of concentration appears when doing experiments in the Water chapter.

Molarity (moles per litre)

The usual way of expressing the concentration of a solution is in moles per litre. This is known as the molarity. A 1 molar solution contains 1 mole of the substance (solute) made up to 1 litre of solution.

Therefore a 1 M solution of sodium hydroxide, NaOH, contains 1 mole of NaOH per litre, or 40g of NaOH per litre of solution (as 40 is the Mr of NaOH). Whereas a 0.5 M solution of NaOH contains 20g of NaOH per litre of solution.

Dilution

When solutions are made in the lab for storage and further use, the solutions are usually concentrated. These solutions are called stock solutions. When a solution of a particular concentration is then required the stock solution is diluted.

Key point: add water and you do dilute the solution, you do change the concentration of the solution but you do NOT change the moles of solute in the solution.
C₁V₁ = C₂V₂

Colour intensity and colorimetry

Some compounds such as potassium manganate(VII) are highly coloured. KMnO₄ is a deep purple colour due to the presence of Mn(+7). When crystals of KMnO₄ are dissolved in a little water the resulting solution is a deep purple colour. However, as more water is added, the colour becomes lighter. The colour intensity is dependent on the concentration. Colour intensity (hence concentration of solute) can be measured using an instrument called a colorimeter. The technique is called colorimetry.

Standard solutions and primary standards

There will always be one standard solution in any titration. This is the one whose concentration is given to you in the question.

Most standard solutions are made from primary standards. An exact amount of the primary standard is weighed out accurately and made up to a known volume in a volumetric flask following certain procedures. As we know the amount of primary standard (solute) and the volume (of solution) used we can calculate the molarity of the solution directly — a titration is not needed to find its concentration, hence it is a standard solution.

Primary standards used in this syllabus: Anhydrous sodium carbonate, Na₂CO₃ (base) and ammonium iron(II) sulfate (reducing agent).
Secondary standards used in this syllabus:
  • NaOH — it is deliquescent (absorbs moisture from the air)
  • KMnO₄ — it decomposes in strong sunlight and is easily reduced, and does not dissolve readily in water
  • I₂ — it undergoes sublimation (changes from solid to gas without going through the liquid stage, hence not stable in air)
  • Na₂S₂O₃ — it is not available in 100% purity

General practical procedures

Preparing a solution (in a volumetric flask)

  1. The solute (if solid) is weighed accurately on a clock glass using an electronic balance.
  2. All of the solute must be transferred to a volumetric flask and made up to an accurately known volume e.g. 250 cm³ so that (a) the concentration can be calculated and (b) there is enough solution to carry out at least 3 titrations.
  3. Solute, if solid, is first tipped into a beaker.
  4. Clock glass is washed several times with deionised water from a wash-bottle and the rinsings are added to the beaker and stirred with a clean dry glass rod until it dissolves.
  5. This solution is poured into the volumetric flask with the aid of a funnel to prevent spillage.
  6. The beaker, funnel and rod are rinsed several times with deionised water and the rinsings are added to the volumetric flask.
  7. The funnel is removed and deionised water is added to the flask until the level of the solution is 1 cm below the mark on the neck of the flask.
  8. Deionised water is then added drop by drop until the bottom of the meniscus is on the graduation mark and read at eye-level.
  9. The solution in the flask has a concentration gradient so the flask is stoppered, inverted 20–30 times and shaken to make the solution homogeneous.
Note: if the solute is in liquid form (vinegar or bleach) we use a properly rinsed pipette to measure out an exact volume, transfer to the volumetric flask by touching the tip to the inside of the flask and then continue as above.
Exam note: In step 9 always say stopper and invert 20–30 times. Marking schemes do not accept 'shake' on its own.

Pipette

  • Used for solutions at room temperature only, as liquids expand on heating and contract on cooling thereby giving inaccurate volumes.
  • Before use the pipette is washed with deionised water to clean it, it is then rinsed with the solution it is to contain to prevent dilution of the solution.
  • Liquid is drawn in using a pipette filler, as solutions may be poisonous, until the level of the liquid is above the mark on the stem of the pipette.
  • The liquid is allowed to run out until the bottom of the meniscus is on the graduation mark and read at eye-level.
  • The solution is transferred to the conical flask by touching the tip of the pipette against the inside of the flask — this ensures the exact volume is transferred.
  • There will be a drop left — do not blow this out as it is accounted for in the calibration of the pipette.

Conical flask (titration flask)

  • Has a narrow neck to prevent spillage while swirling during the titration.
  • Before use the flask is rinsed only with deionised water so that there is no change in the number of reacting moles of solute. If rinsed with the solution to contain there would be an unknown volume of solution in the conical flask and an unknown amount of solute.
  • Flask is swirled continuously during titration.
  • Drops high up on the flask from the burette can be rinsed down using deionised water from a wash bottle as this does not interfere with the number of reacting moles of solute — moles of solute after dilution equals moles of solute before dilution.
  • To observe the colour change a white surface should be placed under the conical flask.

Burette

  • Used for solutions at room temperature.
  • The burette measures to 0.1 cm³ accuracy — its narrowness allows the meniscus to be observed clearly.
  • The burette is clamped vertically using a retort stand to prevent parallax error.
  • In acid–base titrations, the acid is usually placed in the burette, as bases will lead to the tap becoming clogged up (bases react with the glass components to form insoluble substances).
  • Before use, it is rinsed with deionised water to clean it, then rinsed with the solution it is to contain to prevent a change in concentration.
  • Use a funnel with the tap closed to fill above the 0 cm³ graduation mark. When full, the funnel must be removed as drops from the funnel will affect the volume in the burette.
  • The tap is opened to ensure that the bit below the tap is full, so that the first volumes leaving the burette go directly into the conical flask.
  • Readings are taken from the bottom of the meniscus except for KMnO₄ which is taken from the top of the meniscus due to its deep purple colour.

Experiment 1 — Preparing a standard solution of sodium carbonate

Result: 2.65 g of Na₂CO₃ per 250 cm³. Mr of Na₂CO₃ = 2(23) + 12 + 3(16) = 106. Concentration = 2.65 ÷ 106 = 0.025 mol per 250 cm³ = 0.1 mol L⁻¹.
Note: this is not a titration. We are preparing a solution to use in a titration.

Experiment 2 — Standardisation of HCl using standard Na₂CO₃

Indicator: methyl orange (yellow in alkaline solution → pink at end-point)
2HCl + Na₂CO₃ → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂

Typical results

TitrationTitre (cm³)
1st (Rough)19.9
2nd19.6
3rd19.5
Average19.55

Calculation method

HCl: V₁ = 19.55 cm³, M₁ = x, n₁ = 2
Na₂CO₃: V₂ = 25 cm³, M₂ = 0.1 M, n₂ = 1

  1. Moles of Na₂CO₃ reacting = M₂ × V₂ / 1000 = 0.1 × 25 / 1000 = 0.0025 mol
  2. Moles of HCl = 0.0025 × (n₁/n₂) = 0.0025 × 2 = 0.005 mol
  3. Molarity of HCl = 0.005 / (19.55/1000) = 0.256 M
  4. Grams per litre: 0.256 × 36.5 = 9.344 g L⁻¹
Q: Why were only a few drops of indicator used?
A: Indicators are themselves either weak acids or weak bases and will therefore take part in the titration thereby affecting the overall result. To minimise their taking part and having a significant effect, the smallest amount possible is used.

Experiment 3 — Water of crystallisation titration

We use the standardised HCl from Experiment 2 to determine the number of water of crystallisation molecules and % water of crystallisation in hydrated sodium carbonate crystals, Na₂CO₃·xH₂O.

Method for a water of crystallisation calculation

  1. Solve for unknown molarity (M). Answer is always in moles per litre.
  2. See if a litre of solution was used. If not, adjust accordingly (e.g. if 250 cm³ used, divide by 4).
  3. Multiply by Mr of anhydrous form to get grams per volume. (Note: you cannot include the water of crystallisation in the Mr as you do not know how many there are.)
  4. Subtract grams of anhydrous from grams of hydrated given in the question to get grams of water of crystallisation.
  5. To get % of water of crystallisation: (mass of water / mass of hydrated) × 100
  6. To solve for 'x' in the formula: (mass of water / 18) ÷ (mass of anhydrous / Mr of anhydrous)

Worked example

4.3g of washing soda crystals (Na₂CO₃·xH₂O) were dissolved in water and the solution made up to 250 cm³. 25 cm³ of this solution required 15 cm³ of 0.2 M HCl for neutralisation.

2HCl + Na₂CO₃ → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂
  1. Moles of HCl = 0.2 × 15/1000 = 0.003 mol
  2. Moles of Na₂CO₃ = 0.003 / 2 = 0.0015 mol
  3. Molarity = 0.0015 / (25/1000) = 0.06 M
  4. Grams per litre = 0.06 × 106 = 6.36 g L⁻¹
  5. But only made 250 cm³, so divide by 4 = 1.59 g (mass of anhydrous)
  6. Mass of water = 4.30 − 1.59 = 2.71 g
  7. % water of crystallisation = (2.71 / 4.30) × 100 = 63.02%
  8. x = (2.71 / 18) ÷ (1.59 / 106) = 0.1506 / 0.015 = 10

Experiment 4 — Concentration of ethanoic acid in vinegar

This is a weak acid–strong base titration. Indicator: phenolphthalein (colourless → pink)
CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O
Why was the vinegar diluted? The vinegar was too concentrated and would require an excessive amount of sodium hydroxide to react with it.
Unusual setup: In this titration the acid (vinegar) is placed in the conical flask and the base (NaOH) is placed in the burette. This is because the colour change colourless → pink is easier to see than pink → colourless. If the base was in the conical flask the indicator would be pink and a pink to colourless change is more difficult to observe.

Experiment 5 — Standardisation of KMnO₄ (redox titration)

Ammonium iron(II) sulfate — (NH₄)₂SO₄·FeSO₄·6H₂O

  • Is a primary standard, so can be used to standardise potassium manganate(VII).
  • Pipetted into the conical flask.
  • Fe²⁺ is easily oxidised to Fe³⁺ by atmospheric oxygen so ammonium iron(II) sulfate is used to prevent this from happening as it is quite stable in air.
  • Its high molecular mass also ensures accuracy when weighing.
  • Sulphuric acid is added to the volumetric flask to further prevent aerial oxidation of Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺.

Potassium manganate(VII) — KMnO₄

  • Is an oxidising agent, so it itself is reduced in the reaction.
  • Is a secondary standard as it decomposes in strong sunlight and is also easily reduced. It also does not dissolve easily in water.
  • Is a deep purple colour due to Mn(+7).
  • Is always placed in the burette.
  • Titrations are always carried out in acidic conditions to ensure the complete reduction of Mn(+7) to Mn(+2). The sulfuric acid is added to the conical flask.
  • The formation of Mn(+2) acts as an autocatalyst in the reaction.
  • The colour change during the reaction is purple → colourless, as Mn(+7) is purple and Mn(+2) is colourless.
  • If insufficient acid (H₂SO₄) is added to the conical flask the Mn(+7) is incompletely reduced to Mn(+4), which appears as a brown precipitate of MnO₂. To rectify this simply add more sulphuric acid.
  • Neither HCl nor HNO₃ can be used to acidify the solution — HCl will be oxidised by the manganate(VII) solution and HNO₃ is itself a powerful oxidising agent that will take part in the reaction.
MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
End-point: No indicator is needed as the manganate(VII) acts as its own indicator. The end-point is when the colourless solution in the conical flask retains a permanent pink tinge, as there are no more Fe²⁺ present to reduce the purple Mn(+7) to colourless Mn(+2).
Important: The potassium manganate(VII) solution should be used immediately in the next titration as it will decompose and its concentration will change, making the result of the next titration inaccurate.

Experiment 6 — Iron in iron tablets

This experiment involves the same chemistry as Experiment 5 — reacting potassium manganate(VII) with iron(II). This time the iron(II) is in the tablets in the form of iron(II) sulfate. You may be asked to calculate the iron(II) sulfate instead of just iron(II), so read carefully.

MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
Why use 5 tablets? Using more tablets gives a larger mass to measure, reducing the percentage error in weighing. It also produces a solution with enough Fe²⁺ to give a measurable titre.
Why crush the tablets? Crushed tablets dissolve more quickly. A pestle and mortar is used with some dilute sulphuric acid to prevent aerial oxidation of the iron(II).

Experiment 7 — Iodometric titrations (sodium thiosulfate and iodine)

Sodium thiosulfate — Na₂S₂O₃

  • Is a reducing agent, so it itself will be oxidised in the reaction.
  • It reacts with iodine that has been liberated from solutions containing an excess of potassium iodide, KI.
  • It is a secondary standard as it is not available in a pure state.
  • It goes into the burette.

Iodine — I₂

  • It is insoluble in water as it is non-polar and water is a polar solvent.
  • It undergoes sublimation at room temperature to a degree, hence it is a secondary standard as it is not stable in air.
  • A standard solution of iodine is obtained by reacting a standard solution of KMnO₄ (oxidising agent) with excess potassium iodide, KI. It is important that the KI is in excess to (1) ensure that all the oxidising agent is reduced so that the maximum amount of iodine is released and (2) to keep iodine in aqueous solution.
I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻

The iodometric titration procedure

The sodium thiosulfate is placed in the burette. The iodine solution is placed in the conical flask — it is red/brown in colour due to the liberated iodine.

  1. Sodium thiosulfate is added until the solution in the conical flask becomes a straw-yellow colour.
  2. A few drops of starch indicator is then added, which in the presence of iodine turns from straw-yellow to blue-black. (If the starch were added before this stage the blue/black complex is too concentrated and too stable to decompose fast enough to give an accurate end-point.)
  3. More sodium thiosulfate is added until the blue-black colour becomes colourless. This indicates the end-point.
Colours in conical flask: red/brown → straw/yellow → blue/black (starch added) → colourless (end-point)

Experiment 8 — % (w/v) of hypochlorite in bleach

Bleach contains hypochlorite ions, ClO⁻, in the form of NaClO. The hypochlorite ion is an oxidising agent and liberates iodine from solutions of excess potassium iodide in a 1:1 ratio. The amount of hypochlorite ions can be calculated by reacting the liberated iodine with a previously standardised solution of sodium thiosulfate.

ClO⁻ + 2I⁻ + 2H⁺ → Cl⁻ + I₂ + H₂O
I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻
Why was the bleach diluted? The bleach was too concentrated and would require an excessive amount of sodium thiosulfate to react with the iodine that the bleach liberated.
