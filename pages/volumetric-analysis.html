Volumetric Analysis

Volumetric Analysis

At a glance

Core titration logic

The chapter repeatedly comes back to the same four inputs: known concentration, two measured volumes, a balanced equation, and an average titre.

Notes

What volumetric analysis is

Volumetric analysis involves reacting two solutions together to find the concentration of one of them. To know a concentration you need two things: the amount of solute and the amount of solution. Only the solutes react; the solvent does not.

Standard solution – a solution of known concentration.
Primary standard – a solute that is available in 100% purity, is stable in air, has a high molar mass for accuracy, dissolves readily in water, and can be made up directly as a standard solution.
Secondary standard – a solute that cannot be made up directly as an exact standard because it is not available in 100% purity, is unstable in air, has a low molar mass, or does not dissolve readily.
To standardise – to find out the concentration of a solution by titration or colorimetry.

Concentration units

  • % (w/v) = grams per 100 cm³ of solution
  • % (w/w) = grams per 100 g of solution
  • % (v/v) = cm³ per 100 cm³ of solution
  • ppm = 1 mg L⁻¹ for dilute aqueous systems
  • Molarity = moles per litre

General practical procedure

The chapter gives full procedural notes for preparing a solution in a volumetric flask, using a pipette, using a conical flask, and using a burette. Useful exam-safe phrasing:

  • wash with deionised water to clean
  • rinse with the solution to be contained where dilution would matter
  • read the bottom of the meniscus at eye level
  • fill the tip below the burette tap
  • stopper and invert the volumetric flask 20–30 times
Important note: in exam wording, “shake” on its own is too vague — say stopper and invert.

The six titrations in this page

  1. Standardisation of HCl using anhydrous sodium carbonate
  2. Water of crystallisation in Na₂CO₃·xH₂O using standardised HCl
  3. Ethanoic acid in vinegar using NaOH
  4. Standardisation of KMnO₄ using ammonium iron(II) sulfate
  5. Iron in iron tablets using KMnO₄
  6. Iodometric titrations using Na₂S₂O₃ with iodine liberated from KMnO₄ / bleach systems

Acid–base titration highlights

A useful setup is V₁, M₁, n₁ and V₂, M₂, n₂. The consistent method is:

  1. use the known solution to find moles
  2. use the balanced equation to transfer the mole ratio
  3. divide by the measured volume to get the unknown concentration
  4. convert into g L⁻¹ or % (w/v) if required
Indicator logic: use only a few drops, because indicators are themselves weak acids or bases and can affect the result if too much is used.

Redox titration highlights

This chapter places strong emphasis on potassium manganate(VII) as a secondary standard, on acidifying with sulfuric acid, and on the colour change purple → colourless during reaction, with the endpoint being the first permanent pink tinge.

MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
Practical trap: if insufficient sulfuric acid is added, brown MnO₂ appears because Mn(+7) is incompletely reduced. Add more acid if this happens.
