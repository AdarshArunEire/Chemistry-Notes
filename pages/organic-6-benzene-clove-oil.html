Organic Chemistry 6 — Benzene, Steam Distillation and Clove Oil

At a glance

Benzene bonding support diagram

Steam distillation and solvent extraction

Notes

Bonding in benzene

Aromatic compounds contain a ring structure. The ring studied in detail is the benzene ring. Benzene is highly unsaturated, yet it is unexpectedly stable.

  • The six carbon atoms are arranged in a ring structure.
  • Each carbon atom has four electrons in its outermost shell.
  • Three of these electrons form sigma bonds: two to neighbouring carbons and one to hydrogen.
  • This gives the sigma-bonded planar hexagonal skeleton.
  • Each carbon then has one unused electron in a p orbital at right angles to the plane.
  • The p orbitals overlap sideways to form a single ring of charge above and below the ring. These are the delocalised π bonds.
Key idea: because the π electrons are delocalised, the carbon–carbon bonds in benzene are all similar and intermediate between single and double bonds. This explains benzene’s stability and why it tends to undergo substitution, not addition.

Important facts about benzene

  • Benzene is planar and non-polar because the centres of positive and negative charge coincide.
  • It is insoluble in water because it is non-polar and does not form hydrogen bonds with water.
  • It is soluble in non-polar solvents such as cyclohexane.
  • It used to be used as an organic solvent but is no longer used widely because it is highly carcinogenic; methylbenzene (toluene) is used instead.

Range and scope of aromatic chemistry

Examples of important aromatic compounds: aspirin, morphine, paracetamol and penicillin in pharmaceuticals; naphthalene in dyes; heroin and cocaine; DDT; TCP disinfectant; terylene; phenolphthalein; and fragrant oils such as menthol and clove oil.

Extraction of clove oil

Steam distillation uses the principle that when two immiscible liquids are together, their boiling point is lower than either pure liquid alone.

Cloves are unopened flowers, and the oil is at its highest concentration when the heads are pinkish and not ripe. Eugenol is the main constituent of clove oil and contains a benzene ring.

If clove oil were distilled directly from ground cloves, the cloves would need to be heated to their boiling point, causing charring. Steam distillation avoids this damage.

Emulsion – two immiscible liquids together where one is dispersed throughout the other as tiny droplets.

If the distillate forms an emulsion rather than two clear layers, a non-polar solvent such as cyclohexane is used. The cyclohexane dissolves the clove oil but not the water. The organic and aqueous layers are separated in a separating funnel, the organic layer is dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate, then the solvent is evaporated to leave clove oil.

Steam distillation method

  1. Set up the apparatus with steam generator, safety tube, pear-shaped flask, condenser, tubing and receiver.
  2. Add water to the steam generator.
  3. Place the safety tube well below the water level. It releases pressure and helps prevent explosion. The water level should be topped up regularly.
  4. Place a known mass of cloves in the pear-shaped flask with some water.
  5. Pass steam through for about 40 minutes.
  6. The receiver collects a milky clove-oil emulsion.

Solvent extraction steps

  1. Add cyclohexane to the emulsion in the separating funnel.
  2. Shake vigorously, then invert and open the tap to release pressure.
  3. Clamp upright and allow layers to separate.
  4. Draw off the lower aqueous layer and keep the upper organic layer.
  5. Wash the aqueous layer again with more cyclohexane to maximise yield.
  6. Dry the organic layer with anhydrous sodium sulfate until it no longer clumps.
  7. Filter to remove the drying agent and allow cyclohexane to evaporate in a warm place or desiccator / fume cupboard.
Precaution: suck-back can occur when heat is removed. The exam may ask how to prevent it, so this should be labelled and remembered.

Uses of clove oil

  • Flavouring – spiced beef, sweets, pickles.
  • Antiseptic – soap, perfumes.
