Notes

Definition

Rate of reaction
Defn – the rate of a reaction is measured by how the concentration of a reactant or product changes over a given period of time.

Factors affecting rate

Five main factors affect rate:

  • nature of the reactants,
  • particle size,
  • concentration,
  • temperature,
  • catalysts.

Nature of reactants

The notes emphasise that ionic reactions in aqueous solution are usually faster than covalent reactions, because ions are already present and only need to come together, whereas covalent reactions need bonds to break first.

Particle size

Smaller particles give a larger surface area, so more particles are exposed and more effective collisions can occur. The chapter uses the marble-chip / hydrochloric-acid experiment and tracks mass loss as CO₂ escapes.

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
Industrial hazard linked in the notes: very fine powders can cause dust explosions because of huge surface area.

Concentration

The greater the concentration of reactants, the greater the number of particles in a fixed volume and therefore the greater the chance of successful collisions per second.

Temperature

The notes stress two effects of increasing temperature:

  1. particles have greater kinetic energy, and
  2. a greater proportion of particles have activation energy.
Important note. A small temperature rise only slightly increases collision frequency, but it greatly increases the number of particles able to react successfully.

The sodium thiosulfate / hydrochloric acid experiment is used to study temperature, using the cloudiness from sulfur formation.

Na₂S₂O₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + SO₂ + S + H₂O

Catalysts

Catalyst
A substance that alters the rate of a reaction without itself being used up chemically.

The notes include general properties: catalysts are specific, reversible in their action, and can be poisoned.

Heterogeneous catalysis
Reactants and catalyst are in different phases.
Homogeneous catalysis
Reactants and catalyst are in the same phase.
Autocatalysis
The catalyst is a product of the reaction.

Examples named in the chapter include the Haber process, oxidation of methanol on platinum / copper, iodide-catalysed decomposition of hydrogen peroxide, and manganate(VII) / ethanedioic acid.

Activation energy

Activation energy
The minimum energy colliding particles must have in order for a reaction to take place.

The chapter then links activation energy to reaction profile diagrams for exothermic and endothermic reactions.

Mechanisms of catalysis

Two explanations are given:

  • Surface adsorption theory for heterogeneous catalysis.
  • Intermediate compound formation theory for homogeneous catalysis.

The notes also mention the visible colour evidence for an intermediate in the cobalt-catalysed oxidation of potassium sodium tartrate by hydrogen peroxide.

Catalytic converters

This chapter includes catalytic converters in cars as a major applied example. Platinum, palladium and rhodium on a metal honeycomb catalyse conversion of harmful gases such as carbon monoxide and nitrogen oxides into less harmful products.

Worksheet and past-paper focus

This chapter keeps a long question bank on:

  • drawing apparatus for measuring rate,
  • instantaneous rate from graphs,
  • effect of concentration / particle size / temperature,
  • activation energy diagrams, and
  • heterogeneous versus homogeneous catalysis.
