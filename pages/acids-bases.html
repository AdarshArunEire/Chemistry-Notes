Acids & Bases

At a glance

Acid definitions at a glance

Strong vs weak dissociation

Notes

In this section of our course we will study both acids and bases. Both of these substances occur around us in our every day lives. For example, indigestion is caused by the over production of acid in our stomachs and to relieve this we take a base such as Milk of Magnesia. Bee stings are acidic and can be neutralised by using baking soda paste. Shampoos are slightly basic and we use conditioner that is slightly acidic to neutralise the effects of the shampoo.

Arrhenius theory of acids and bases

Acid (Arrhenius)
Defn – An acid produces H⁺ ions in water.
Base (Arrhenius)
Defn – A base produces OH⁻ ions in water.

Problems with Arrhenius’ theory: it assumes independent H⁺ ions in solution and is restricted mainly to aqueous solutions. In practice the hydrogen ion is hydrated as H₃O⁺ (hydronium / oxonium ion).

Alkali – an alkali is a base that is soluble in water.
General acid dissociation: HA + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + A⁻
Example: HNO₃ + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + NO₃⁻

Monobasic, dibasic and tribasic acids

Monobasic acid
Produces one H⁺ ion in solution.
Dibasic acid
Produces two H⁺ ions in solution.
Tribasic acid
Produces three H⁺ ions in solution.

Typical syllabus examples are HCl (monobasic), H₂SO₄ (dibasic) and H₃PO₄ (tribasic).

Brønsted–Lowry theory

Acid (Brønsted–Lowry)
Defn – an acid is a proton donor.
Base (Brønsted–Lowry)
Defn – a base is a proton acceptor.
Amphoteric
Defn – a substance that can act as both an acid and a base.

Water can act as both an acid and a base, so it is amphoteric.

Conjugate pairs

Conjugate pair
Defn – two species that differ by one proton.
Conjugate acid
Defn – a base plus a proton.
Conjugate base
Defn – an acid minus a proton.
NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

In this example NH₃ / NH₄⁺ and H₂O / OH⁻ are conjugate pairs.

Dissociation equations

When acids are placed in solution they split into hydrogen ions and anions. The number of dissociation steps depends on whether the acid is mono-, di- or tribasic.

HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
CH₃COOH + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + CH₃COO⁻
H₂SO₄ + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + HSO₄⁻
HSO₄⁻ + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + SO₄²⁻
H₃PO₄ + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + H₂PO₄⁻ ⇌ HPO₄²⁻ ⇌ PO₄³⁻

Reactions of acids with metals and bases

These must be learned well: acids react with metals to give a salt and hydrogen; with alkalis and bases to give a salt and water; with carbonates / hydrogencarbonates to give a salt, water and carbon dioxide.

acid + metal → salt + H₂
acid + alkali → salt + H₂O
acid + carbonate → salt + H₂O + CO₂

Neutralisation and salts

Neutralisation reaction
Acid reacts with alkali / base to produce a salt and water.
Salt
Defn – A salt is formed when the hydrogen of the acid is replaced by a metal ion or NH₄⁺.

The non-metal part of a salt is called the radical or complex ion.

Strong and weak acids / bases

Strong acid
Brønsted–Lowry: a good proton donor. Arrhenius: dissociates completely in water.
Weak acid
Brønsted–Lowry: a poor proton donor. Arrhenius: does not dissociate completely in water.

Strong acids have weak conjugate bases; weak acids have stronger conjugate bases. On this course the classic weak acid example is ethanoic acid.

Strong base
Brønsted–Lowry: a good acceptor of protons. Arrhenius: almost completely dissociates in water to give OH⁻ ions.
Weak base
Brønsted–Lowry: a poor proton acceptor. Arrhenius: only slightly dissociates in water.

Household examples

  • Ethanoic acid in vinegar.
  • Citric acid in oranges and lemons.
  • Carbonic acid in fizzy drinks.
  • Ammonia solution in cleaning agents.
  • Magnesium hydroxide in indigestion remedies.
  • Zinc oxide in skin creams.

Worksheet / past-paper focus

This chapter includes a long worksheet and Leaving Cert question run from 2003 to 2024. The recurring themes are definitions, conjugate pairs, distinguishing strong vs weak acids, dissociation equations, and explaining why NH₃ is a Brønsted–Lowry base but not an Arrhenius base.

Quick answer outlines

Definitions

Define (i) Brønsted–Lowry acid, (ii) Brønsted–Lowry base, (iii) conjugate acid–base pair.

Show answer outline
  • Acid: a proton donor.
  • Base: a proton acceptor.
  • Conjugate acid–base pair: two species that differ by one proton.
Ammonia

Explain why NH3 is a base according to Brønsted–Lowry but not according to Arrhenius.

Show answer outline
  • Brønsted–Lowry: NH3 accepts a proton.
  • Arrhenius: it does not dissociate in water to produce OH− ions directly.
Strong and weak acids

How does a strong acid differ from a weak acid?

Show answer outline
  • Strong acid: good proton donor / large degree of dissociation.
  • Weak acid: poor proton donor / only partly dissociated.
Conjugate pairs

Identify the conjugate acid–base pairs in HCO3− + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 + OH−.

Show answer outline

Pairs: HCO3− / H2CO3 and H2O / OH−.

Relative acid strength

Why is 0.10 M H2SO4 lower in pH than 0.10 M HCl, and why is 0.10 M HCOOH higher in pH than 0.10 M HCl?

Show answer outline
  • H2SO4: diprotic, so it produces more H+ ions.
  • HCl: strong monoprotic acid.
  • HCOOH: weak acid, so it is only partly dissociated.
Indicator choice

Name a suitable indicator for a strong acid–weak base titration and explain why it is suitable.

Show answer outline
  • Suitable indicator: methyl orange / methyl red.
  • Why: the end point is below pH 7 and the indicator range lies within the steep part of the titration curve.
