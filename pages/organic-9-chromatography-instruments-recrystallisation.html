Chromatography

This is a technique that is used to separate different substances in a mixture. You can also then identify and measure the amount of these substances present in the mixture using other instruments.

The principle

The basic principle is that a mobile phase (a solvent) will carry a dissolved mixture through a stationary phase (paper or thin layer of aluminium oxide). The separation occurs as some of the components of the mixture tend to be held (attracted to) by the stationary phase and move more slowly than those that mix well with the mobile phase.

Experiment — to separate a mixture of indicators (or inks) using chromatography

Using paper chromatography

Theory behind the experiment – a small amount of the indicator mixture is placed on a strip of filter paper. This paper is placed so that it just touches a suitable solvent. The solvent rises through the paper, the mixture dissolves, and the components move at different rates. Those that do not dissolve well in the solvent stay lower on the paper; those that dissolve better are carried further.

Rf value
Rf = distance travelled by component / distance travelled by solvent front

These values may be used to tentatively identify the components in a mixture of indicators.

Apparatus: chromatography paper, gas jar, ruler, pencil, sellotape, glass rod, hair dryer, capillary tubes.

Materials: a mixture of indicators, water / ethanol / ammonia solution (solvent).

Method

  1. Add solvent to the gas jar to a depth of about 1 cm and allow the tank to become saturated with vapour.
  2. Draw a line near the top and another near the bottom of the paper in pencil.
  3. Place a small spot of mixture on the bottom line using a capillary tube and dry it with a hair dryer. Repeat to build up a concentrated spot.
  4. Place the paper into the gas jar with the spot side down so that the end of the paper just touches the solvent. Do not immerse the spot.
  5. Allow the chromatogram to run until the solvent reaches the top line, then remove and dry the paper.
Note: the particles of the stationary phase in paper chromatography are not small and uniform in size, so the results are less accurate than TLC.

Questions on the experiment

  • Why is the gas jar not used for a time after solvent has been added? To allow the tank to become saturated with solvent vapour.
  • Why are two lines drawn? One indicates where the sample starts; the other indicates the distance travelled by the solvent front so the Rf value can be calculated.
  • When is it possible to separate two components? When one component is attracted to significantly different extents by the stationary phase and / or mobile phase.
  • If two substances have different Rf values under the same conditions, what does that mean? The two substances are not identical.

Using thin layer chromatography (TLC)

TLC plates have the stationary phase as a layer of aluminium oxide or silica gel spread thinly and evenly over aluminium foil. It is more efficient than paper chromatography because the particles in the stationary phase are smaller and more uniform in size, giving a better and more accurate separation.

Method summary: line at top and bottom of the plate, place sample spot on bottom line, dry it, stand the plate in a solvent-saturated tank with the spot above the solvent level, allow the solvent to rise, then remove and dry the plate.

  • Why is filter paper placed around the walls of the gas jar? To speed up saturation of the tank with solvent vapour.
  • When is it possible to separate components? When one component is attracted to significantly different extents by the stationary phase and / or mobile phase.
  • If two substances have identical Rf values? They may be the same, but further evidence is needed to establish this conclusively.

Social and applied aspects of TLC: used in the pharmaceutical industry to determine purity of drugs, and in forensic science to separate colours in dyes extracted from fibres.

Using column chromatography

The stationary phase, silica gel, is placed in a long glass tube (the column). Glass wool at the end prevents the material from running out. The solvent is the eluent, and passing the solvent through the column is called elution.

Apparatus: syringe, adapter, extraction column.

Materials: mixture of indicators, methanol, water.

Method

  1. Flush the solid phase extraction column with methanol, then with water.
  2. Place a sample of the mixture on top of the column to a depth of about 1 mm.
  3. Add the methanol solution to the syringe and force it through the column.
  4. The different components separate as different coloured bands and are collected in separate beakers.
  • Purpose of the syringe: to force the solvent through the column under pressure.
  • Why is an adapter necessary? To fit the syringe exactly into the column.
  • Why flush with methanol then water? Methanol removes residual organic material; water removes remaining methanol.

Instrumental methods of separation and analysis

Mass spectrometry – revise from atomic theory.

Gas chromatography (GC) – a gas is the mobile phase. The stationary phase is a high boiling point liquid spread on silica gel packed into a long coiled tube. The sample is injected, vaporised, and carried through by an unreactive gas such as nitrogen or helium. Each component leaves the column at a different time and the detector records peaks on a gas chromatogram. GC is used to measure alcohol in blood or urine and in drug-test samples from athletes.

High performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) – used to determine non-volatile components of a mixture. Very small solid-phase particles are used, so pressure is required. It is used in food analysis, for example growth promoters in meat and vitamins in food.

Infra-red spectrometry (IR) – infrared radiation is invisible. Organic compounds absorb infrared radiation; the bonds vibrate, producing a fingerprint that can be checked against a database. IR is used to identify plastics and drugs.

Ultra-violet spectrometry (UV) – when a substance absorbs ultraviolet light an electron may be promoted to a higher energy level. Maximum absorption tends to occur at a certain wavelength, helping to identify the compound and its concentration. It is used in the quantitative determination of organic compounds such as drug metabolites and plant pigments.

These techniques are applicable to many other areas of chemistry, not just organic chemistry.

Experiment — recrystallisation of benzoic acid and determination of its melting point

This technique is used to separate and purify organic solids. It works on the principle that more solute is soluble in a hot solvent than in a cold solvent. The substance to be purified is dissolved in the minimum amount of hot solvent possible and then filtered quickly to remove any insoluble impurities. As the filtrate cools, crystals appear. These are isolated and then tested for purity by measuring their melting point.

Apparatus: beaker, Buchner funnel, vacuum pump, filter paper, melting point tubes, thermometer, Bunsen burner, clock glass.

Materials: benzoic acid, liquid paraffin.

Method

  1. Place 5 g of impure benzoic acid crystals in a beaker.
  2. Dissolve them in the minimum amount of hot water.
  3. First filtration: filter the hot concentrated solution through a Buchner funnel under suction. Insoluble impurities remain on the filter paper.
  4. Allow the filtrate to cool slowly and fully.
  5. Second filtration: filter off the excess solvent. The crystals remain on the filter paper and are allowed to dry.

Determination of melting point

  1. Tap some crystals into a melting point tube until about 0.5 cm of crystals are at the end of the tube.
  2. Attach the tube to a thermometer using a rubber band.
  3. Half-fill a boiling tube with liquid paraffin and place the tube and thermometer into it.
  4. Heat while stirring the paraffin thoroughly throughout.
  5. Note the temperature at which the crystals start to melt.
  6. Allow the paraffin to cool about 10 °C, then repeat more slowly with a fresh sample.
Purity rule: if the crystals are pure then a sharp melting point is obtained. If the crystals are impure then a lower and broader melting-point range is obtained.

Cracking

This is a process that involves breaking up long chain hydrocarbons for which there is low demand into short chain hydrocarbons for which there is high demand. This can be done using a catalyst or heat, i.e. catalytic cracking or thermal cracking.

It is important to remember that for every bond that is broken a double bond must be formed in a product molecule. For example, if a molecule is cracked in two places then there will be three products and two of them will contain a carbon–carbon double bond.

Why is TLC usually better than paper chromatography?

Column chromatography

Show answer outline
  • Syringe: forces solvent through the column under pressure.
  • Adapter: gives a tight fit between syringe and column.
  • Methanol wash: removes organic residue.

Recrystallisation and melting point

What are the key points to mention when asked about the purification of benzoic acid?

Show answer outline
  • Dissolve the solid in the minimum amount of hot solvent.
  • Filter hot to remove insoluble impurities.
  • Cool slowly to allow crystals to form.
  • Filter, wash and dry the crystals.
  • Pure sample: sharp melting point close to the correct value.
  • Impure sample: lower and broader melting-point range.
