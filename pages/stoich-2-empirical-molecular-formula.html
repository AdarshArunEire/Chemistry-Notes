Empirical formula and molecular formula

Molecular formula – this formula shows the actual number and type of atoms present in a molecule.

Example: C2H4 shows that there are 2 atoms of carbon and 4 atoms of hydrogen present in a molecule of ethene.

Empirical formula – this is the simplest whole-number ratio showing the atoms present in a molecule.
  • Empirical formula of C2H4 = CH2
  • Empirical formula of C4H10 = C2H5
  • Empirical formula of C6H12O6 = CH2O

Question type 1 — empirical formula from percentage composition

Question: What is the empirical formula for the compound that contains 42.1% sodium, 18.9% phosphorus and 39.0% oxygen?

Method:

  1. Assume 100 g.
  2. Divide each of the grams by the Ar to get the moles.
  3. Take the smallest number of moles and divide it into itself and into everything else to get the simplest whole-number ratio. Adjust if necessary to get whole numbers.
  4. This is the empirical formula.

Na – 42.1 g / 23 = 1.83 moles → 1.83 / 0.61 = 3

P – 18.9 g / 31 = 0.61 moles → 0.61 / 0.61 = 1

O – 39.0 g / 16 = 2.44 moles → 2.44 / 0.61 = 4

Empirical formula = Na3PO4

Note – there was no need to adjust, as they were all whole numbers already after dividing.

Question type 2 — empirical formula from experimental results

Question: On reduction, 0.58 g of an oxide of silver yielded 0.54 g of the pure metal. What is the empirical formula of the oxide? (LCH 1992)

Method:

  1. Find the grams of each element from the information in the question.
  2. Divide each of the grams by the Ar to get the moles.
  3. Take the smallest number of moles and divide it into itself and into everything else to get the simplest whole-number ratio.
  4. This is the empirical formula.

Mass of oxygen = 0.58 g − 0.54 g = 0.04 g

Ag – 0.54 / 108 = 0.005 moles → 0.005 / 0.0025 = 2

O – 0.04 / 16 = 0.0025 moles → 0.0025 / 0.0025 = 1

Empirical formula = Ag2O

Question type 3 — molecular formula

The molecular formula is a multiple of the empirical formula so find the empirical formula first.

Method:

  1. Calculate the empirical formula first.
  2. Find the molecular mass of the empirical formula.
  3. Divide the molecular mass of the molecular formula (given in the question) by the Mr of the empirical formula.
  4. The molecular formula will be this many times the empirical formula.

Question: An organic compound consists of 36.36% carbon, 3.03% hydrogen, 32.32% oxygen and 28.28% nitrogen by mass. Find its empirical formula. If its relative molecular mass is 198 find its molecular formula. (LCH 1991)

C: 36.36 / 12 = 3.03 → 3.03 / 2.02 = 1.5 × 2 = 3

H: 3.03 / 1 = 3.03 → 3.03 / 2.02 = 1.5 × 2 = 3

O: 32.32 / 16 = 2.02 → 2.02 / 2.02 = 1 × 2 = 2

N: 28.28 / 14 = 2.02 → 2.02 / 2.02 = 1 × 2 = 2

Empirical formula = C3H3O2N2

Mr of empirical formula = 99

198 / 99 = 2

Molecular formula = C6H6O4N4

Question type 4 — combustion analysis

When a compound containing carbon and hydrogen is completely combusted in a suitable apparatus, all of the carbon is converted to carbon dioxide and all of the hydrogen to water. We can then use the masses of carbon dioxide and water to determine the amounts of carbon and hydrogen in the original sample and hence find the empirical formula / molecular formula.

Question: A mass of 11.6 mg of a saturated organic liquid, containing the elements carbon, hydrogen and oxygen, gave 26.4 mg of carbon dioxide and 10.8 mg of water on complete combustion. Find the molecular formula of the liquid. (LCH 1996)

Method:

  1. Convert mg to grams (if necessary) by dividing by 1000.
  2. Find grams of C in carbon dioxide by multiplying by the mass fraction of carbon in carbon dioxide.
  3. Repeat to find grams of H in water.
  4. Add grams of C and H together and subtract from the total mass of the compound to get the grams of O.
  5. Find the empirical formula in the usual manner.
  6. Use the mass and moles of compound to find the Mr of the molecular formula.

Fraction of C in CO2 = 12/44. So 0.0264 g CO2 contains 0.0072 g C.

Fraction of H in H2O = 2/18. So 0.0108 g H2O contains 0.0012 g H.

Mass of O in original compound = 0.0116 − (0.0072 + 0.0012) = 0.0032 g.

Mole ratio C:H:O = 3:6:1 so empirical formula = C3H6O.

0.0002 moles have mass 0.0116 g, so Mr = 58.

Mr of empirical formula is also 58, so molecular formula = C3H6O.

Further sample question

Isopropyl alcohol, a substance sold as rubbing alcohol, is a compound known to contain C, H and O. Combustion of 0.255 g of isopropyl alcohol produces 0.561 g of carbon dioxide and 0.306 g of water. Find the empirical formula of the alcohol.

Answer outlines for the questions above

Empirical formula practice

Manganese oxide: When 6.87 g of an oxide of manganese was reduced, 4.95 g of the metal was obtained. Find the empirical formula of the oxide.

Show answer outline
  • Mass of oxygen: 6.87 − 4.95 = 1.92 g.
  • Moles: Mn = 4.95 ÷ 55 = 0.09 mol; O = 1.92 ÷ 16 = 0.12 mol.
  • Ratio: 0.09 : 0.12 = 3 : 4.
  • Empirical formula: Mn3O4.
Leaving Cert 2005

When 3.175 g of copper reacts with chlorine gas, 6.725 g of copper chloride is formed. Find by calculation the empirical formula of the chloride.

Show answer outline
  • Mass of chlorine: 6.725 − 3.175 = 3.55 g.
  • Moles: Cu = 3.175 ÷ 63.5 = 0.050 mol; Cl = 3.55 ÷ 35.5 = 0.100 mol.
  • Ratio: 1 : 2.
  • Empirical formula: CuCl2.
Leaving Cert 2013–2015

Use the same empirical-formula method for the copper oxide, sulfur oxide and copper nitride questions listed above.

Show answer outline
  • 2013 copper oxide: CuO.
  • 2014 sulfur oxide: SO3.
  • 2015 copper nitride: Cu3N.
  • Method: convert masses or percentages to moles, divide by the smallest value, then write the simplest whole-number ratio.
Leaving Cert 2018

Epoxyethane is composed of 54.5% carbon, 9.1% hydrogen and 36.4% oxygen by mass. Find the empirical formula.

Show answer outline
  • Moles: C = 54.5 ÷ 12 = 4.54, H = 9.1 ÷ 1 = 9.1, O = 36.4 ÷ 16 = 2.275.
  • Divide by the smallest: C : H : O = 2 : 4 : 1.
  • Empirical formula: C2H4O.
Further sample question

Use the combustion-analysis method for the isopropyl alcohol question at the end of the chapter.

Show answer outline
  • Find the mass of carbon from the carbon dioxide.
  • Find the mass of hydrogen from the water.
  • Subtract from the total sample mass to get the mass of oxygen.
  • Convert all three masses to moles and divide by the smallest value.
  • Empirical formula: C3H8O.
