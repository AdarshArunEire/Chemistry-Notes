The Octet Rule, Valency and Formula Writing

The forces that combine elements together are called chemical bonds.

AN ELEMENT
Defn- an element is defined as a substance that cannot be reduced to other simpler substances by chemical means or produced by combining simpler substances.

This definition was put forward by Sir Robert Boyle, an Irish scientist in the early 1900s. We will meet Boyle again when we study gases.

THE OCTET RULE
Defn- This rule states that when atoms bond, most atoms tend to want an electron arrangement of eight electrons in their outermost shell (energy level).
Note - The octet rule is a guide used to help work out formulae of compounds. It must be noted that it is just a guide and there are many exceptions to the octet rule e.g. Be and B.

Noble gases

The noble gases do not often form chemical bonds with other atoms as they have eight electrons in their outermost energy level and hence are very stable on their own. (He being stable with it’s 2 electrons in it’s one and only shell (energy level)).

Question – do all noble gases have full outer shells?
Answer – No, He and Ne do but the others have 8 electrons in their outer shell which makes them stable.
VALENCY
Defn- the valency of an atom tells us the number of chemical bonds an atom can form.

The number of electrons in the outermost shell determines the valency. Atoms will either share, lose/gain or share electrons to attain a full ‘octet’. For example, elements in group 1 have one electron in their outermost shell but would like 8. It is easier for elements in group 1 to lose or share one electron to attain a full octet than gain 7 electrons, hence group 1 elements have a valency of 1!

Elements in group 5 however will usually gain or share 3 electrons rather than lose 5 electrons so elements in group 5 have a common valency of 3! ‘Common’ means that other valencies are possible also but we will start with the most common.

Writing Chemical Formulas

Using the octet rule to predict the formulae of simple compounds whose names end in -IDE. Usually only two elements in these compounds.

Worked example — magnesium chloride

Example- What is the formula of magnesium chloride?

Solution-
The valency of Mg is 2 as it is in group 2.
The valency of Cl is 1 as it is in group 7.
The valencies must balance therefore for every ONE Mg atom we will need TWO chlorine atoms.

Answer – MgCl2
Show solution

Answer: Mg has valency 2 and Cl has valency 1, so one Mg needs two Cl atoms. Formula: MgCl2.

Worked example — hydrogen sulphide

Example - What is the formula of hydrogen sulphide?

Solution-
The valency of H is 1 as it is in group 1.
The valency of S is 2 as it is in group 6.
The valencies must balance therefore for every TWO hydrogens we need just ONE sulphur atom.

Answer – H2S
Show solution

Answer: H has valency 1 and S has valency 2, so two H atoms are needed for each S atom. Formula: H2S.

Worked example — aluminium oxide

Example – What is the formula of aluminium oxide?

Solution –
The valency of Al is 3 as it is in group 3.
The valency of O is 2 as it is in group 6.
The valencies must balance ( the LCM of 3 and 2 is 6) so for every TWO aluminium atoms we will need THREE oxygen atoms.

Answer – Al2O3
Show solution

Answer: Al has valency 3 and O has valency 2, so the simplest balanced ratio is 2 : 3. Formula: Al2O3.

Formulae of compounds containing transition elements

Transition elements (along with elements in groups 4,5 and 6) have variable (more than one) valencies, so you must know the valency of the element before you can work out the formula. The valency of the transition element is given using a roman numeral in the name.

Worked example — copper(I) oxide

Example- What is the formula of copper (I) oxide?

Solution –
The valency of Cu is 1 (from the roman numeral).
The valency of O is 2 as it is in group 6.
The valencies must balance so TWO copper atoms are needed for every ONE oxygen atom.

Answer – Cu2O
Show solution

Answer: Cu(I) means valency 1; oxide is valency 2. Two Cu atoms balance one O atom. Formula: Cu2O.

Worked example — copper(II) oxide

Example – What is the formula of copper (II) oxide?

Solution –
The valency of Cu is 2 (from the roman numeral).
The valency of O is 2 as it is in group 6.
The valencies must balance so ONE copper atom for every ONE oxygen atom.

Answer – CuO
Show solution

Answer: Cu(II) means valency 2 and oxide is valency 2, so the ratio is 1 : 1. Formula: CuO.

Note- all the above compounds end in –ide as they all contain just two elements.

Other compounds formulae are more obvious from their names

  • Carbon dioxide – one carbon to TWO (di) oxygens: CO2
  • Carbon monoxide – one carbon to one oxygen: CO
  • Nitrogen monoxide – one nitrogen to ONE (mono) oxygen: NO
  • Nitrogen dioxide – one nitrogen to two oxygens: NO2
  • Sulfur dioxide – one sulfur to two oxygens: SO2
  • Sulphur trioxide - one sulphur to THREE (tri) oxygens: SO3
  • Phosphorous (V) chloride – one phosphorus to five chlorines: PCl5
  • Dinitrogen tetroxide? N2O4

Writing Chemical Formulas Using Complex Ions / Radicals

Formulae of compounds containing complex ions (radicals) whose names end in –ATE or – ITE

* exceptions as do not end in ‘ate’ or ‘ite’.

A complex ion is an ion containing more than one element. As there are quite a few to remember it is important that you learn off the following table!!

Once you have memorised the valances of these thirteen complex ions it will now be easy to write the formula of more complicated compounds. Remember that in the Leaving Cert chemistry exam you will be expected to be able to write formula for compounds just as in the Leaving Cert English exam you are expected to know the alphabet!!

Note – most of the above radicals end in –ate or -ite, which means oxygen is part of the ion.

Worked example — sodium sulphate

Example - What is the formula of sodium sulphate?

Solution –
The valency of Na is 1 as it is in group 1.
The valency of the sulphate ion is 2 (memorised!).
The valencies must balance so you need TWO sodium atoms for every ONE sulphate ion.

Answer – Na2SO4
Show solution

Answer: Na has valency 1 and sulfate has charge 2−, so two Na+ are needed. Formula: Na2SO4.

Worked example — calcium hydrogencarbonate

Example - What is the formula of calcium hydrogencarbonate?

Solution –
The valency of Ca is 2 as it is in group 2.
The valency of the hydrogencarbonate ion is 1 (memorised!).
The valencies must balance so for every ONE calcium you need TWO hydrogencarbonate ions. N.B. As you need more than one of the radical you must put the radical in brackets and the quantity outside the brackets.

Answer – Ca(HCO3)2
Show solution

Answer: Ca is 2+ and hydrogencarbonate is 1−, so two hydrogencarbonate ions are needed. Use brackets around the radical. Formula: Ca(HCO3)2.

Worked example — copper(II) nitrate

Example – What is the formula of copper (II) nitrate?

Solution –
The valency of Cu is 2 (from the roman numeral).
The valency of nitrate is 1 (memorised!).
The valencies must balance so for every ONE copper atom you need TWO nitrate ions (remember brackets around the nitrate as there is more than one of them!)

Answer – Cu(NO3)2
Show solution

Answer: Cu(II) is 2+ and nitrate is 1−, so two nitrate ions are needed. Use brackets around nitrate. Formula: Cu(NO3)2.

Finally formulas that may catch you out!!

  • Ammonia - NH3
  • Methane gas – CH4 (this is an organic compound- completely different set of rules for naming! Will learn this in Organic Hand out 1)
  • Phosphine gas - PH3
  • Ozone gas - O3
  • Water - H2O (just in case!)
