Organic Chemistry 3 — Chloroalkanes and Alcohols

At a glance

Chloroalkane family and alcohol family

Primary, secondary and tertiary alcohols

Notes

Chloroalkanes

Chloroalkanes do not occur naturally. They are mainly used as solvents to remove oil or grease in the dry cleaning industry and as solvents in the paints industry. They are also used as coolants in fridges and air-conditioning units and as propellants in aerosols. The notes also state that they destroy the ozone layer so their use is being reduced.

CHLOROALKANE
Defn – A chloroalkane is an alkane where one or more of the hydrogen atoms have been replaced by chlorine atoms.

Simple examples

NameFormulaComment
ChloromethaneCH₃Clsingle chlorine substitution
DichloromethaneCH₂Cl₂two chlorines
TrichloromethaneCHCl₃common name: chloroform; early anaesthetic and non-polar solvent
TetrachloromethaneCCl₄fully chlorinated methane

Naming larger chloroalkanes

  1. Select the longest carbon chain to give the parent alkane.
  2. Number from whichever end gives the lowest possible number to the carbon atom to which the chlorine atom is attached.
  3. Use the appropriate prefix – di, tri, tetra, if more than one chlorine is attached.
Prompt note: the original notes say “Examples – Name and draw the following molecules (a) (b) (c)” but the actual displayed molecules are missing in the notes provided, so they have not been invented here.

Properties of chloroalkanes

  • Chloroalkanes are non-polar, so they are insoluble in water which is a polar solvent.
  • They are soluble in non-polar solvents such as cyclohexane.
  • They are used as non-polar solvents themselves in dry cleaning as they remove oil-based stains.
  • They have relatively low boiling points due to weak van der Waals forces of attraction between molecules.
  • They have been proven to cause major damage to the ozone layer.

Alcohols

ALCOHOL
Defn – an alcohol is an alkane where one of the hydrogens is replaced with an –OH group.
  • The –OH group is called the hydroxyl group and this is the functional group of alcohols.
  • The general formula of the alcohols is CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH.
  • All of the carbon atoms are tetrahedral in shape and the –OH group is V-shaped.
  • The –OH group is polar while the rest of the carbon chain is non-polar.

Naming alcohols

  1. Replace the –ane ending of the corresponding alkane with –anol, i.e. methanol, ethanol, propanol etc.
  2. Choose the longest carbon chain and name from the end that gives the carbon atom with the –OH group attached the smallest possible number. It is the position of the hydroxyl functional group that determines the direction.

Classification of alcohols

For example ethanol, propan-1-ol and butan-1-ol are primary alcohols. Propan-2-ol and butan-2-ol are secondary alcohols. The chapter also asks: draw 2-methylpropan-2-ol and classify this alcohol. That compound is tertiary.

Note: tertiary alcohols are not easily oxidised.

Ethanol

Ethanol is the best known alcohol as it is found in alcoholic drinks such as beer, wine and spirits. Ethanol is produced by a process called fermentation. Sugars are converted to ethanol and carbon dioxide by yeast. Yeast contains the enzyme zymase.

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂    (zymase)

All alcohols are toxic and are broken down in the body by the liver to an aldehyde (primary metabolite).

Uses of ethanol

  • Alcoholic drinks.
  • Solvent for perfumes.
  • Can be used to sterilise.
  • In Brazil over 20% of cars run on ethanol (oxygenate).
Health note : Consumption of alcohol to excess can lead to liver failure and eventually death.

Boiling points, states of matter and solubility of alcohols

Alcohols have higher boiling points than alkanes of similar molecular mass because the polar –OH group allows hydrogen bonding between alcohol molecules. More energy is needed to break these bonds, hence a higher boiling point.

This hydrogen bonding also accounts for the fact that the first four members of the alcohols are liquids. (Remember that the first four members of the alkanes are gases due to van der Waals forces.)

The alcohols with smaller carbon chains are soluble in water because there is hydrogen bonding between alcohol molecules and water molecules. However, as the carbon chain increases in length, the non-polar carbon chain takes over from the polar –OH group so solubility decreases. The larger alcohols are insoluble in water but are readily soluble in non-polar solvents such as cyclohexane.

Remember – all alkanes, chloroalkanes and alcohols have tetrahedral carbon because each carbon in these molecules forms four single bonding pairs and no lone pairs.

Reactions of alcohols

The chapter notes that these are learned in more detail in later handouts where balanced equations are written. The summary list is:

  1. Alcohols can act as acids with extremely reactive metals such as sodium to form a salt and hydrogen gas.
  2. Primary alcohols can be oxidised to aldehydes and then on to form carboxylic acids. Secondary alcohols can be oxidised to ketones. The oxidising agent used is acidified sodium dichromate.
  3. Alcohols react with carboxylic acids to form esters and water. This is called an esterification reaction or a condensation reaction.
  4. Alcohols can undergo elimination reactions (dehydration) to form an alkene and water.
