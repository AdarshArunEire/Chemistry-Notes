Atomic Theory 7 — Ionisation Energy

At a glance

Study diagram — general ionisation energy trends

Study diagram — why successive ionisation energies jump

Successive ionisation energies rise every time, but the biggest jump happens when the next electron would come from a more stable inner shell.

Ionisation energies

First ionisation energy – the minimum amount of energy required to remove the first most loosely bound electron from a mole of isolated atoms of an element in their neutral gaseous ground state.
X(g) → X+(g) + e−

Trends in the periodic table – always use the table in the log book.

General trends

Across a period

  • There is an increase in ionisation energy on going across a period due to an increase in nuclear charge and no change in the screening effect.
  • This leads to a decrease in atomic radius.
  • The outer electrons are more firmly held thus more energy is needed to remove the electrons.

Down a group

  • There is a decrease in ionisation energy on going down a group due to an increase in atomic radius caused by extra shells being added.
  • This leads to inner completed shells screening out some of the nuclear attraction.
  • The outer electrons are further away from the nucleus and the nuclear charge is blocked out by inner completed shells, thus the outer electrons are less firmly held and less energy is needed to remove them.
Use these four headings in answers: these four headings should be considered whenever answering ionisation energy questions.

The four headings to consider

1. Radius size (distance from the nucleus)

The smaller the atomic radius, the greater the energy needed to remove the electron as the positive nucleus will have a great hold on the electron. The larger the radius, the amount of energy needed to remove the electron decreases.

2. Stability of energy level (shell)

The noble gases will have the highest first ionisation energies as they have eight electrons in their outer shell, which leads to increased stability. (He is stable with 2 electrons in its only shell.)

The alkali metals (Group 1) will have the lowest first ionisation energies as they only have one electron in their outermost shell and will lose it very easily to attain a stable configuration.

Full shells are very stable and shells with eight electrons are also stable so more energy will be needed to remove electrons from these arrangements.

3. Stability of sublevel

A full sublevel is the most stable one, the next most stable is a half-filled one.

The most stable p configuration is a full one, i.e. 6 electrons. The next most stable p configuration is a half-filled one, i.e. 3 electrons.

Therefore it requires less energy to remove an electron from an unfilled sublevel than from a full sublevel or a half-filled sublevel, because these latter configurations have extra stability.

4. Removing from an atom versus an ion

Second and successive ionisation energies are always higher.

When removing the second electron it will require more energy as you are now removing from an ion as opposed to an atom. While the nuclear charge has not changed, the ion has less electrons so the remaining electrons are held more strongly.

The ionisation energies of successive electrons become higher again due to the same fact.

Chemical properties of elements

The chemical properties of the elements depend on the arrangement of the electrons in the atoms. All elements in Group 1 have one electron in their outermost shells and therefore have similar chemical properties.

Sample question (HLC 1988)

Question: Write the electronic configuration (s, p) for atoms of nitrogen, phosphorous and sulphur. Account for the decrease in first ionisation energy (a) from nitrogen to phosphorous, (b) from phosphorous to sulphur.

  • N: 1s² 2s² 2p³
  • P: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p³
  • S: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴

Decrease from N to P: P has a larger atomic radius than N as P has an extra shell. As a result, the outermost electron in P is less firmly held than the outermost electron in N. Therefore less energy is needed to remove it.

Decrease from P to S: P has a half-filled outer p sublevel that is more stable than the outer sublevel in S. Therefore it requires less energy to remove the outermost electron from S than from P.

Note – always refer to the elements by name or symbol in the question. Try to avoid using the word “it” as this can lead to uncertainty.

Sample question (HLC 1996)

Question: The values of the first four ionisation energies for scandium and titanium are shown in a table. From which electron sublevel is each electron being removed?

  • First electron lost in Sc from the 4s sublevel as it is of lower energy than 3d.
  • First electron lost in Ti from the 4s sublevel as it is of lower energy than 3d.
  • Third electron lost in Sc from the 3d sublevel.
  • Third electron lost in Ti from the 3d sublevel.

Ti has a greater nuclear charge than Sc and therefore a smaller atomic radius. Thus it requires more energy to remove Ti’s first three electrons than Sc’s first three electrons.

The fourth electron in Sc is being removed from an extremely stable and full outermost 3p sublevel, hence it requires a lot of energy to remove it. By comparison, the fourth electron removed from Ti is still being removed from the 3d sublevel.

Check list for questions involving ionisation energy

  • Distance of the electron from the nucleus (radius).
  • Stability of the sublevel from which the electron is being removed, i.e. full or half-filled s, p, d etc. sublevel.
  • Stability of the shell from which the electron is being removed, i.e. n = 1 or n = 2 etc. (if it applies).
  • Whether the electron is being removed from an ion or an atom.

If you consider these points when attempting a question it should help. Remember, you have to decide which factors apply in each question. Pay particular attention to the marking scheme.

  • Occupied orbitals: 4.
  • Boundary unclear: Heisenberg uncertainty principle / wave nature.

  • First ionisation energy: the minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron completely from an isolated gaseous atom of an element in its ground state.

  • Si < C: more shells, more shielding, larger radius.

These are grouped here as exam-practice material so the recurring question types can be revised together.

  • Use periodic table / log table data to account for peaks and dips in first ionisation energy graphs.
  • Define first ionisation energy and second ionisation energy.
  • Explain why sodium’s second ionisation energy is dramatically higher than its first.
  • Account for the dramatic increase between particular successive ionisations of magnesium or carbon.
Show answer outline
  • First ionisation energy: the minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron completely from an isolated gaseous atom of an element in its ground state.
  • Second ionisation energy: the minimum energy required to remove the second most loosely bound electron completely from each ion in one mole of gaseous 1+ ions of an element.
  • Trends and comparisons: use effective nuclear charge, shielding and atomic radius every time. Across a period ionisation energy generally increases; down a group it generally decreases. Large jumps in successive ionisation energies show different energy levels, and small dips are explained by sub-level effects or paired electrons.
