Electrochemistry

At a glance

Notes

Core definition and opening idea

Electrode potential – the electrode potential of a metal is a measurement of the tendency of a metal to lose electrons i.e. the ability of the metal to be oxidised.

The chapter opens by linking electrochemistry with reactivity: the more easily a metal loses electrons, the more reactive / easily oxidised it is.

Voltaic cell

A voltaic cell uses a spontaneous chemical reaction to generate an electric current. In a standard example, zinc and copper are immersed in suitable electrolytes. Zinc is oxidised and copper ions are reduced.

Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻     Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

The difference in oxidation tendency gives the electrical energy of the cell. A typical voltage is about 1.1 V for the Zn/Cu cell.

Electrolyte – a substance that can conduct electricity in the molten or dissolved state. In practice it is often an ionic compound or salt solution.

Hydrogen half-cell

To measure the individual tendency of a metal to lose electrons, chemists compare it with the standard hydrogen half-cell.

Hydrogen half-cell – platinum electrode (inert), hydrogen gas bubbled over the electrode, and H⁺ ions in solution.

This acts as the reference half-cell against which metal electrode potentials are measured.

The electrochemical series

Electrochemical series – a list of metals in order of their ability to lose electrons or be oxidised.
  • Metals at the top lose electrons readily, are highly reactive and form stable compounds.
  • Metals at the bottom lose electrons less readily, are less reactive and can sometimes be found free in nature.
  • Metals at the top displace those lower down from solutions of their salts.

Displacement reactions in solution

Displacement reaction – a reaction where a metal takes the place of a less reactive metal from a solution of its salts.

This chapter uses examples such as magnesium in copper(II) sulfate and iron extracting copper from a salt solution.

Mg + CuSO₄ → MgSO₄ + Cu

Because magnesium is higher in the electrochemical series, magnesium is oxidised and copper(II) ions are reduced. The chapter notes that the magnesium becomes coated with copper.

Electrolysis

Electrolysis – a process that uses electricity to bring about a chemical reaction.

Electrolysis is the reverse of a spontaneous voltaic cell: an external power source drives a non-spontaneous reaction. This chapter includes electrolysis using inert electrodes and the electrolysis of water / aqueous solutions as worked examples.

General rules: oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction occurs at the cathode.

Purification of copper

Copper purification is a core application of electrolysis.

  • The impure copper is the anode.
  • The pure copper sheet is the cathode.
  • Copper dissolves from the anode as Cu²⁺ and is deposited on the cathode as copper metal.
  • Less reactive impurities collect as anode sludge.
Anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻     Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Practice questions

This chapter includes displacement and electrolysis questions, including electrolysis of aqueous potassium iodide with phenolphthalein, electrolysis of water, Hoffmann voltameter questions, copper purification, and recent Leaving Cert questions about sodium sulfate and KI electrolysis.

Important practical note: for aqueous KI with phenolphthalein and inert electrodes, iodine forms at one electrode and hydrogen / hydroxide form at the other, which explains the colour changes near each side.

Definitions

Define (i) electrode potential, (ii) electrolysis, (iii) displacement reaction.

Show answer outline
  • Electrode potential: a measure of the tendency of a metal to lose electrons.
  • Electrolysis: use of electricity to bring about a chemical reaction.
  • Displacement reaction: a reaction where a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from a solution of its salts.
Hydrogen half-cell

What is the purpose of the standard hydrogen half-cell?

Show answer outline

Answer: it acts as the reference half-cell against which other electrode potentials are measured.

Electrochemical series

What does the electrochemical series tell you?

Show answer outline
  • It lists metals in order of ease of oxidation / electron loss.
  • Metals higher in the series are more reactive.
  • A metal higher in the series displaces one lower down from solution.
Copper purification

What happens at each electrode during purification of copper?

Show answer outline
  • Anode: impure copper, Cu → Cu2+ + 2e−.
  • Cathode: pure copper sheet, Cu2+ + 2e− → Cu.
  • Anode sludge: less reactive impurities collect here.
Electrolysis of aqueous KI

What products form when aqueous potassium iodide is electrolysed using inert electrodes?

Show answer outline
  • Anode: iodine forms.
  • Cathode: hydrogen gas forms.
  • In solution: OH− builds up near the cathode, so phenolphthalein turns pink there.
