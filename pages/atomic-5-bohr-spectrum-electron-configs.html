Niels Bohr (1885–1962)

Bohr, a Danish physicist, came up with a model to illustrate the way electrons are arranged in an atom. Prior to this point, electrons were thought to be able to possess any amount of energy (so they could be found anywhere around the nucleus). Bohr showed that this was not the case and the amount of energy an electron possessed was of a definite quantifiable amount. He showed that the electron can exist in certain stable states and these stable states are governed by a quantum number called ‘n’. n=1 is the first and is the closest to the nucleus of the atom. As ‘n’ increases the energy states become progressively closer to one another. (n = shell number)

Electrons are found in shells surrounding the nucleus.

What Bohr noticed when he carried out his experiment

When white light is passed through a prism the light is split up into an array of colours called the spectrum. This is known as the continuous spectrum since it consists of a continuous range of wavelengths (colours). The most familiar example of such a spectrum is the rainbow.

Bohr carried out experiments using light coming from a hydrogen discharge tube.

When he passed the light coming out from the discharge tube through a prism, only lines of a few wavelengths (colours) were present in the resultant spectra. The coloured lines were separated by black regions, which correspond to wavelengths (colours) that were absent in the light.

He saw a series of lines and therefore called it an emission line spectrum.

He repeated this experiment with other elements and noticed that each element had its own characteristic line spectrum (fingerprint, hence a means of identification).

The fact that the spectrum consisted of a series of lines indicated to him that only certain energy emissions are possible.

What Bohr concluded from his experiment

In an atom, electrons revolve around the nucleus in certain allowed orbits or shells. When in a shell, an electron has a definite amount of energy.

An energy level is one of the discrete amounts of energy that an electron has when it is in an atom. It is dependent on the distance from the nucleus. When close to nucleus the electron has little energy, when far from nucleus the electron has more energy.

When an electron stays in a particular energy level the amount of energy it contains remains the same.

When an atom absorbs energy, the electrons jump from a lower to a higher energy level. This ‘excited’ state is unstable and temporary.

The excited electron will fall back to a lower energy level and as it does energy is given off. As the electron can only fall back to certain definite energy levels, only fixed amounts of energy (quantifiable amounts) can be given off.

Atomic emission spectrum of hydrogen

Hydrogen’s only electron occupies the lowest available energy level – ground state.

When energy is given to the electron by heating it for example, the electron is promoted to a higher energy level. This state is unstable and temporary.

When the electron falls back to a lower level, the energy difference is emitted as a photon of light. E2 – E1 = E

Each line in the spectrum is the result of the electron moving from one energy level to a lower one.

Each transition has a definite energy and appears as a line of a particular colour in the line emission spectrum. Hence each line has a definite frequency. This indicates that only a limited number of energy changes are possible within the structure of an atom.

E = hf    where E = energy, h = Planck’s constant, f = frequency
f = 1/λ    where λ = wavelength
  • When electrons fall from a higher energy level to the n=1 energy level, we get a set of lines called the Lyman series. These exist in the ultraviolet band.
  • When electrons fall from a higher energy level to the n=2 energy level, we get a set of lines called the Balmer series (this is what we get in the atomic emission spectrum of hydrogen as it is in the visible band).
  • When electrons fall from a higher energy level to the n=3 energy level, we get a set of lines called the Paschen series. These exist in the infra red band.
Note – it is important that you learn the above atomic spectrum of hydrogen off by heart as you will see that it regularly appears in exam questions.

Why do different elements have unique atomic spectra?

Each element has a different arrangement of energy levels and a different electronic configuration, giving rise to different electron transitions (jumps) from higher to lower energy levels.

Explain why different metals produce different flame colours

Different metals have different numbers of electrons and therefore arrangements of electrons.

Therefore, different electron transitions occur from one energy level to another.

Each transition has a definite amount of energy, thus frequency, thus wavelength.

Seeing as wavelength equals colour we get different colours of light being emitted from different elements (metals).

Atomic absorption spectrum

Atoms can also absorb light.

If white light is passed through an element in its gaseous form, the light that comes out has wavelengths missing. These missing wavelengths appear as dark lines and have the same energy as would appear in the emission spectrum.

The amount of light absorbed is proportional to the concentration of the element. This is why AAS is used to determine the quantity of ‘heavy metals’ present in water analysis and the amount of lead in a blood sample.

Therefore atoms in the ground state can absorb the same amount of energy as they would emit in the excited state.

Social and applied aspects

  • AAS (atomic absorption spectrometry) – used to analyse the amount and type of elements present in a sample (quality control).
  • Street lights – sodium streetlights give out light with a distinct colour (amber).
  • Fireworks – the elements used in fireworks give out their distinct colours when they are heated.

Limitations of Bohr’s Theory

According to Bohr’s theory an electron moves in a fixed path or orbit around the nucleus, which works when dealing with hydrogen as it only has one electron.

  1. This theory failed when applied to atoms with more than one electron i.e. all others.
  2. Does not take wave-particle duality into account.
  3. Does not allow for uncertainty (probability).
  4. Does not explain the discovery of sublevels.

Louis Victor de Broglie proposed in 1923 that electrons may behave as waves as well as particles, like light. Proven by experiment 4 years later.

Erwin Schrodinger in 1926 published the first paper on wave mechanics in which the electron in atoms was modelled as a wave rather than as a particle. (See Schrodinger’s cat.)

2018 saw a series of lectures called Schrodinger 75 in Dublin to mark the 75th anniversary of a series of lectures given here by this Austrian Noble prize winning scientist. His ‘What is Life?’ series was delivered in 1943 at a time when the question of heredity was a major one in science. Schrodinger lived in Ireland for 17 years and set up the Institute for Advanced studies in Dublin. He became an Irish citizen in 1948.

HEISENBERG’S UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE
Defn - This states that it is impossible to know both the position and the speed (velocity) of an electron at the same time as electrons move in a wave motion.

The mass of an electron is so small that they are disturbed by the measuring techniques and this must be taken into account. The more precise one measurement the less precise the other. So we are left with dealing with the probability of finding an electron at a particular position within an atom.

ATOMIC ORBITAL
Defn – an atomic orbital is a region in space around the nucleus of an atom in which there is a high probability of finding an electron.
ENERGY LEVEL
Defn - is the discrete (exact) amount of energy an electron has when it is in an atom.
ENERGY SUBLEVEL
Defn- a group of atomic orbitals within an atom, all of which have the same energy.

Electronic configurations of atoms and ions

The electronic configuration of an element shows you the energy levels, sublevels and orbitals present in a particular element and the location of the electrons in these.

i.e. 1s 2s 2p 3s 3p 4s 3d 4p 4d etc.

It is important to note that the p sublevel is split in three orbitals, also that these orbitals are of equal energy. Also that the 4s sublevel is of lower energy than the 3d sublevel, so it gets filled first.

Examples:

  • Na atom: look up the atomic number to see how many electrons the Na atom has. 11 e → 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s1
  • Na ion: 11 e − 1 e = 10 e → [Na]+ = 1s2 2s2 2p6
  • N atom: 7 e → 1s2 2s2 2p3 (Note – half filled p-sublevel is very stable)
  • Br atom: 35 e → 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 4s2 3d10 4p5
  • Sulfide ion: 16 e + 2 e = 18 e → [S]2− = 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6

REMINDER – the atomic number is the number above the symbol in the periodic table! It is always a whole number.

s, p and d-blocks

  • s-block elements - All the elements in group I and II have their highest energy electron in an s orbital hence these elements are known as the s-block elements.
  • p-block elements - All elements in groups III to 0 have their highest energy electron in a p-orbital hence these are known as p-block elements.
  • d-block elements - The elements in-between groups II and III are known as the d-block elements as their highest energy electron enters a d orbital.

This occurs because half-filled and full sublevels are more stable than partially filled sublevels. Remember that these are the only two exceptions on your syllabus that do this!

TRANSITION ELEMENT
Defn – an element that forms at least one ion with a partially filled d-sublevel.

Mandatory experiment – flame tests

Theory behind the experiment – different metals emit different colour flames to the Bunsen burner due to each metal having different electron arrangements.

Apparatus – safety glasses, Bunsen burner, nichrome (or platinum) wire.

Materials – concentrated hydrochloric acid (two different batches), salts of sodium, lithium, copper, potassium, calcium.

Method –

  1. The nichrome wire was first cleaned by dipping it in concentrated hydrochloric acid (batch one).
  2. It was then heated to red hot in the Bunsen flame until no flame colour was observed.
  3. The wire was then dipped into a clean sample of hydrochloric acid (batch two) and then into the salt to be tested.
  4. The wire was then held in the Bunsen flame.
  5. The colour of the flame was noted and recorded.
  6. This procedure was repeated for the other salts, ensuring that the wire was cleaned thoroughly each time.
