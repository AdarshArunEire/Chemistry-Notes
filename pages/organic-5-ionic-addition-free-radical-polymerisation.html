Propagation: Cl• + CH₄ → HCl + CH₃•   ;   CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl•

Termination: Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂   ;   CH₃• + Cl• → CH₃Cl   ;   CH₃• + CH₃• → C₂H₆

Notes

Ionic addition reactions

Definition – Ionic addition reactions occur when two substances react together to form one main product.

Alkenes typically undergo ionic addition reactions. Substances add across the double bond to form the main product of the reaction. Consider addition to an ethene molecule.

Addition to ethene

  • Hydrogenation: Ethene + hydrogen → ethane. This is also called hydrogenation and is used industrially to convert unsaturated oils to margarine using a nickel catalyst at about 200 °C.
  • Halogenation with chlorine: Ethene + chlorine → 1,2-dichloroethane. This product is used later to make chloroethene (vinyl chloride) and then PVC.
  • Bromine test: Ethene + bromine → 1,2-dibromoethane. Bromine water is red-brown and goes colourless with an unsaturated compound such as ethene.
  • Addition of hydrogen chloride: Ethene + HCl → chloroethane.
  • Hydration: Ethene + water → ethanol.
C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆    |    C₂H₄ + Cl₂ → C₂H₄Cl₂    |    C₂H₄ + Br₂ → C₂H₄Br₂    |    C₂H₄ + HCl → C₂H₅Cl    |    C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH

Mechanism of ionic addition using ethene and bromine

  1. Stage A: the C=C bond is an area of negativity because four electrons are being shared. This polarises the approaching bromine molecule.
  2. Stage B: the bromine molecule splits into Br⁺ and Br⁻. This is heterolytic fission because the molecule splits to produce two different ions.
  3. Stage C: Br⁺ attacks the double bond, a dative covalent bond forms, the double bond becomes single, and the other carbon becomes positively charged. This intermediate is the carbonium ion (carbocation).
  4. Stage D: Br⁻ attacks the positive carbonium ion to give 1,2-dibromoethane.
Evidence for the ionic-addition mechanism: if bromination is carried out in sodium chloride solution, the major product is still 1,2-dibromoethane, but trace 1-bromo-2-chloroethane and 2-bromoethanol also form. These side-products support the existence of the carbonium ion intermediate because Cl⁻ or water can attack it.

When chlorine adds across the double bond, Cl⁺ attacks first and then Cl⁻ attacks the carbonium ion to form 1,2-dichloroethane. When HCl adds, H⁺ attacks first and then Cl⁻ attacks the carbonium ion to form chloroethane.

Important exam wording: the 2007 exam required the term ionic addition to describe the reaction between chlorine and ethene. “Addition” on its own was not accepted.

Polymerisation reaction

Definition – A polymer is a very long chained molecule made when many thousands of small-chained molecules called monomers join together.

Polymerisation of ethene is an addition polymerisation reaction. Many ethene molecules add together to form polyethene (polythene), used in plastic bags, cling-film, lunch boxes and squeezy bottles.

Propene undergoes a similar process to form polypropene, found in items such as plastic jugs, beakers and luggage.

Free radical substitution reaction

Definition – A free radical substitution reaction occurs when an atom or group of atoms is replaced by another atom or group of atoms. Two products are formed.

Substitution reactions are the typical reactions of alkanes. On this course the key example is the halogenation of alkanes, where a group 7 element replaces hydrogen in an alkane. The required condition is ultraviolet light.

Mechanism: monochlorination of methane

  1. Initiation: Cl₂ is broken by UV light into two chlorine radicals. This is homolytic fission because each atom keeps one bonding electron.
  2. Propagation: a chlorine radical attacks methane to give HCl and a methyl radical; the methyl radical then attacks Cl₂ to form chloromethane and regenerate a chlorine radical. Because a new radical is formed, a chain reaction occurs.
  3. Termination: radicals combine with each other. Examples: Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂, CH₃• + Cl• → CH₃Cl, CH₃• + CH₃• → C₂H₆.

Also important is the monochlorination of ethane, where the propagation stage gives an ethyl radical and chloroethane, and termination can also give butane.

Evidence supporting the free-radical mechanism: the reaction does not occur without UV light; one absorbed photon leads to many molecules of product, showing a chain reaction; products such as ethane / butane form by radical combination; and radical promoters such as tetramethyl lead or tetraethyl lead speed up the reaction.

Past-paper focus

  • Role of UV light in chlorination of methane.
  • Why traces of ethane or butane support the free-radical mechanism.
  • Bromination of ethene and the evidence for ionic addition.
  • Repeating unit of polyethene or PVC synthesis sequence.
