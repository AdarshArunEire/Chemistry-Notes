pH framework

pH 7 is neutral only at 25 °C. Pure water is neutral at all temperatures, but the neutral pH value changes with temperature because Kw changes.

Notes

Ionic product of water

Starting with the self-ionisation of water:

H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻
or
H₂O + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻
Kw
Defn – Kw is the product of the concentrations of hydrogen ions and hydroxide ions in water.

At 25 °C, Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴. In pure water at 25 °C, [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ mol L⁻¹.

Important: pure water is neutral at all temperatures because [H⁺] always equals [OH⁻]. What changes is the value of Kw, so the pH of neutrality is not always 7.

Definition of pH

pH
Defn – pH is minus the log to base 10 of the hydrogen ion concentration, where [H⁺] is expressed in mol per litre.
pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]

Also relevant is pOH and the relationship pH + pOH = 14 at 25 °C.

Strong acids and strong bases

For strong acids, the approach is to write the dissociation equation, use the molar ratio to find [H⁺], then apply the pH formula.

Example pattern: H₂SO₄ → 2H⁺ + SO₄²⁻

For strong bases, write the dissociation equation, find [OH⁻], calculate pOH, then use pH = 14 − pOH.

Checklist: make sure units are in mol L⁻¹, write the dissociation equation, use the molar ratio, then substitute into the correct formula.

Weak acids and weak bases

Note that weak acids and bases do not fully dissociate, so the full balanced-equation molar ratio cannot be used directly.

Weak-acid approximation
[H⁺] ≈ √(Ka × c)
Weak-base approximation
[OH⁻] ≈ √(Kb × c)

This is the method repeatedly used in the worked examples and worksheet questions.

What to do when given the pH

Also important is reversing the process: start from pH, calculate [H⁺], then work backwards to the acid concentration using the dissociation / stoichiometric relationship.

Indicators

Indicator function is explained using the standard weak-acid equilibrium idea:

HIn ⇌ H⁺ + In⁻

The undissociated and dissociated forms have different colours. Added acid pushes the equilibrium left; added base reduces [H⁺] and shifts the equilibrium right.

Range of an indicator
The pH range over which there is a noticeable colour change.

Choosing an indicator for a titration

  • Strong acid + strong base: a wide pH jump, so several indicators work.
  • Strong acid + weak base: choose an indicator that changes in the acidic region.
  • Weak acid + strong base: choose an indicator that changes in the alkaline region.
  • Weak acid + weak base: no ordinary indicator gives a sharp visible end-point.

Worksheet and past-paper coverage

  • calculating pH of strong and weak acids / bases,
  • working backwards from pH to concentration,
  • indicator colour and range, and
  • choosing an indicator from a titration curve.

Concentration from pH

What is the hydrogen ion concentration of a solution with pH 5.6?

Show answer outline
  • [H+] = 10−5.6.
  • Answer: about 2.5 × 10−6 mol L−1.
Effect of dilution

A strong monobasic acid is diluted tenfold. What happens to the pH?

Show answer outline

Answer: the pH increases by 1.

Indicators

An indicator is represented by HA ⇌ H+ + A−, where HA is yellow and A− is blue. What colour is seen in a strong base?

Show answer outline
  • Colour: blue.
  • Why: OH− removes H+, so equilibrium moves to the right and A− dominates.
Indicator choice from a curve

How do you decide whether an indicator is suitable for a titration curve?

Show answer outline
  • Check whether the indicator range lies within the steep vertical part of the curve.
  • If it does, the indicator is suitable.
