Notes

Atomic theory definitions

Element – substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.
Metal – element that loses electrons to form a positive ion.
Molecule – a neutral particle made up of two or more atoms joined together.
Atomic radius – half the distance between the centres of singly bonded atoms of the same element.
Emission spectrum – a series of coloured lines against a dark background.
Absorption spectrum – a series of dark lines against a coloured background.
Atomic number – the number of protons present in the nucleus of an atom.
Mass number – the mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.
Isotopes – isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same atomic number but different mass numbers due to the different numbers of neutrons present in the nucleus.
Relative atomic mass – the average mass of an atom of an element compared to 1/12 the mass of the carbon-12 isotope, taking relative abundances of isotopes into account.
Relative molecular mass – the average mass of a molecule of a compound compared to 1/12 the mass of the carbon-12 isotope, taking relative abundances of isotopes into account.
Radioactivity – the spontaneous disintegration of the nucleus with the emission of α, β or γ radiation.
Nuclear reaction – a reaction involving a change in the nucleus of an atom; a new element is usually formed.
Chemical reaction – a reaction involving electrons being shared or transferred; the nucleus remains the same.
Half-life – the time taken for half the nuclei in a given sample to decay.
Heisenberg's uncertainty principle – it is impossible to know both the position and the speed of an electron at the same time.
Atomic orbital – a region in space around the nucleus of an atom in which there is a high probability of finding an electron.
Energy level – a discrete amount of energy an electron has when it is in an atom.
Transition element – an element that forms at least one ion with a partially filled d-subshell.
d-block element – an element whose highest-energy electron occupies a d-subshell.
Aufbau principle – electrons occupy the lowest available energy level.
Hund's rule of maximum multiplicity – equal-energy orbitals are filled singly before electrons pair.
Pauli exclusion principle – no more than two electrons can occupy an orbital and they must have opposite spin.
First ionisation energy – the minimum energy required to remove the first most loosely bound electron from a mole of isolated atoms in the gaseous ground state.

Stoichiometry definitions

Molecular formula – actual number and type of atoms in a molecule.
Empirical formula – simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a molecule.
A mole – that amount of a substance which contains Avogadro's constant number of particles.
Avogadro's constant – the number of carbon atoms in exactly 12 g of the carbon-12 isotope.
Avogadro's law – equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.
Molar mass – the atomic or molecular mass expressed in grams.
Molar volume – at s.t.p. one mole of any gas occupies 22.4 litres (22,400 cm³).
Limiting reactant – the reactant that is fully consumed when a reaction goes to completion.
Theoretical yield – the quantity of product that should form when all of the limiting reactant reacts.
Actual yield – the quantity of product actually obtained from a reaction.
Percentage yield – the actual yield divided by the theoretical yield, multiplied by 100.

Acids and bases definitions

Acid (Arrhenius) – an acid produces H⁺ ions in water.
Monobasic acid – produces one H⁺ ion in solution.
Dibasic acid – produces two H⁺ ions in solution.
Tribasic acid – produces three H⁺ ions in solution.
Base (Arrhenius) – a base produces OH⁻ ions in water.
Acid (Brønsted–Lowry) – a proton donor.
Base (Brønsted–Lowry) – a proton acceptor.
Amphoteric – a substance that can act as both an acid and a base.
Conjugate pair – two species that differ by one proton.
Salt – formed when the H of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or NH₄⁺.
Strong acid – a good proton donor; dissociates completely in water.
Weak acid – a poor proton donor; does not dissociate completely in water.
Conjugate acid – a base plus a proton.
Conjugate base – an acid minus a proton.
Alkali – a base that is soluble in water.
Strong base – a good proton acceptor; almost completely dissociates in water to produce OH⁻ ions.
Weak base – a poor proton acceptor; only slightly dissociates in water to produce OH⁻ ions.

Bonding definitions

Compound – a substance made up of two or more elements combined chemically.
Octet rule – most atoms tend to want eight electrons in the outermost shell when bonding.
Valency – the number of chemical bonds an atom can form.
Ionic bond – electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions caused by complete electron transfer.
Sigma bonding – orbitals overlap head-on / end-on.
Pi bonding – orbitals overlap side-ways.
Pure covalent bonding – electrons are shared equally.
Polar covalent bonding – electrons are shared unequally.
Dative covalent bonding – one atom supplies both electrons in the covalent bond.
Polar molecule – the slight positive and slight negative poles are separated by a distance.
Electronegativity – the relative power of attraction of an atom for the shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond.
Hydrogen bonding – an intermolecular force of attraction that occurs when hydrogen is bonded to a small electronegative atom such as O, F or N.
Van der Waals forces – weak intermolecular forces caused by temporary dipoles.
Dipole-dipole interactions – weak intermolecular forces between polar molecules.

Oxidation and reduction definitions

Oxidation (electron transfer) – loss of electrons.
Reduction (electron transfer) – gain of electrons.
Oxidation number – the charge an element has or appears to have when rules are applied.
Oxidation (oxidation number) – increase in oxidation number.
Reduction (oxidation number) – decrease in oxidation number.
Redox reaction – a reaction in which oxidation and reduction both occur.
Oxidising agent – a substance that causes oxidation and is itself reduced.
Reducing agent – a substance that causes reduction and is itself oxidised.

Volumetric-analysis definitions

Standard solution – a solution of known concentration.
Primary standard – a solute available in 100% purity, stable in air, high molar mass, readily soluble, and usable to prepare a standard solution directly.
Secondary standard – not available in 100% purity / not stable / low molecular mass / not readily soluble, so it cannot be made directly to a standard concentration.
Standardise – find the concentration of a solution by titration or colorimetry.
Solution – a mixture made up of a solute and a solvent.
Solute – the substance dissolved in a solvent.
Solvent – the substance that dissolves the solute.
Concentration – amount of solute per amount of solution.
Molarity – concentration expressed in moles per litre.

Shapes, thermochemistry, organic and equilibrium definitions

Valence-shell electron-pair repulsion theory – outer-shell electron pairs arrange themselves as far apart as possible.
Lone pair of electrons – an outer-shell pair that does not take part in bonding.
Bonding pair of electrons – an outer-shell pair shared between atoms.
Heat of reaction (ΔH) – the heat change that occurs when a reaction takes place according to a given balanced chemical equation.
Exothermic reaction – heat is lost from the reaction to the surroundings; ΔH is negative.
Endothermic reaction – heat is taken in by the reaction from the surroundings; ΔH is positive.
Heat of combustion – heat change when 1 mole of a substance is burned in excess oxygen.
Kilogram calorific value – heat produced when 1 kilogram of fuel is burned in excess oxygen.
Heat of formation – heat change when 1 mole of a substance is formed from its elements in their standard states.
Hess's law – the sum of the heat changes of individual stages equals the heat change of the overall reaction.
Law of conservation of energy – energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.
Bond energy – the energy required to break 1 mole of covalent bonds and separate the neutral atoms completely.
Heat of neutralisation – the heat change when 1 mole of H⁺ ions from an acid reacts with 1 mole of OH⁻ ions from a base to form water.
Hydrocarbon – a compound consisting of carbon and hydrogen only.
Structural isomers – same molecular formula but different structural formulae.
Homologous series – group of compounds with the same functional group; successive members differ by CH₂.
Functional group – group of atoms on which the characteristic properties of a homologous series depend.
Chloroalkane – alkane where one or more H atoms are replaced by chlorine atoms.
Reforming – changing straight-chain hydrocarbons into branched-chain or cyclic hydrocarbons.
Cracking – changing long-chain hydrocarbons into short-chain hydrocarbons.
Auto-ignition / knocking – early explosion of a petrol-air mixture caused by pressure in the engine.
Octane number – measure of a fuel's tendency to resist knocking.
Free radical – an atom or group of atoms with an unpaired electron.
Carbonium ion / carbocation – a positively charged carbon intermediate.
Alcohol – an alkane where one hydrogen atom is replaced by an –OH group.
Saturated – single bonds only around carbon atoms.
Unsaturated – has a multiple bond between carbon atoms.
Aromatic – contains a benzene ring.
Aliphatic – straight or branched chained molecule.
Ionic addition reaction – two reactants form one main product.
Free radical substitution reaction – an atom or group of atoms is replaced, forming two main products.
Condensation reaction – two different molecules form a more complex molecule and a smaller molecule such as water.
Esterification reaction – an alcohol and carboxylic acid react reversibly to form an ester and water.
Elimination reaction – a smaller molecule is removed from a larger one; a double bond forms.
Dehydration reaction – removal of water from a molecule.
Hydrogenation reaction – addition of hydrogen to an alkene using Ni and 200 °C.
Heterolytic fission – a molecule splits to form two different ions.
Homolytic fission – a covalent bond breaks evenly so that each atom keeps one electron, forming free radicals.
Polymerisation reaction – many small molecules called monomers combine to form a polymer.
Chemical equilibrium – the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction.
Dynamic equilibrium – forward and reverse reactions occur at the same time; both are ongoing.
Le Chatelier's principle – an equilibrium system alters to oppose the effect of an applied stress.
Reversible reaction – both forward and reverse reactions happen.

Gas laws, electrochemistry, rates, water and principles definitions

Boyle's law – at constant temperature, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to pressure.
Charles's law – at constant pressure, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
Ideal gas – a gas that obeys all gas laws under all conditions of temperature and pressure.
Gay-Lussac's law of combining volumes – reacting gas volumes bear a simple whole-number ratio under the same conditions of temperature and pressure.
Electrolyte – a substance that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved.
Electrochemical series – list of elements arranged in order of tendency to lose electrons / be oxidised.
Electrode potential – a measure of the tendency of a metal to lose electrons / be oxidised.
Displacement reaction – a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution.
Electrolysis – using electricity to bring about a chemical reaction.
Rate of reaction – change in concentration, per unit time, of any one reactant or product.
Catalyst – a substance that alters the rate of reaction without being used up.
Heterogeneous catalysis – catalyst and reactants are in different phases.
Homogeneous catalysis – catalyst and reactants are in the same phase.
Autocatalysis – the catalyst is a product of the reaction.
Activation energy – minimum energy colliding particles must have for reaction to occur.
pH – minus the log to base 10 of the hydrogen-ion concentration.
Indicator – a substance that changes colour depending on the pH of the solution.
Range of an indicator – the pH range over which there is a noticeable colour change.
Kw / ionic product of water – product of hydrogen ion and hydroxide ion concentrations in water.
Hard water – water that does not form a lather easily with soap.
Temporary hardness – hardness that can be removed by boiling; caused by calcium and magnesium hydrogencarbonates.
Permanent hardness – hardness that cannot be removed by boiling; caused by calcium and magnesium sulfates.
Biochemical oxygen demand – amount of dissolved oxygen consumed by biochemical action when a sample is kept in the dark for 5 days at 20 °C.
Mass spectrometer (principle) – separation of ions based on different masses when moving in a magnetic field.
Fractional distillation (principle) – separation based on different boiling points.
Steam distillation (principle) – two immiscible liquids together have a lower boiling point than either liquid alone.
Chromatography (principle) – separation because mixture components are attracted to the mobile and stationary phases to different extents.
Recrystallisation (principle) – more solute is soluble in a hot solvent than in a cold one.
Colorimetry (principle) – concentration of solute is proportional to colour intensity of solution.
Note: these cover the main definitions in the course, but you should still add any extra terms you meet during the year.
