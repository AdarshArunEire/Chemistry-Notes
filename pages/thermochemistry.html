Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry — Heats of Reaction, Hess’s Law, Calorimetry

At a glance

Exothermic vs endothermic

Bomb calorimeter

Notes

Some chemical reactions can result in changes in temperature, for example heat can be given out or heat can be taken in. In this section of our course we are going to examine these heat changes and some reaction types in which this happens.

HEAT OF REACTION (ΔH)
Defn – The heat change that occurs when a reaction takes place according to a given balanced chemical equation.

Exothermic reaction

In an exothermic reaction heat is given out from the reaction to the surroundings as the reaction loses heat. The heat of reaction is negative (ΔH = −).

Examples – all combustion reactions are exothermic, the Haber Process for the manufacture of ammonia is exothermic, the reaction that occurs when concentrated sulfuric acid is mixed with water.

Endothermic reaction

In an endothermic reaction heat is taken in from the surroundings as the reaction gains heat. The heat of reaction is positive (ΔH = +).

Examples – the reaction of ammonium nitrate with water and the formation of ethyne gas.

Different names can be given to heats of reactions depending on what happens in the reaction. The main ones are heat of combustion, heat of formation and heat of neutralisation.

Heat of combustion

HEAT OF COMBUSTION
Defn – The heat change that occurs when 1 mole of a substance is burned in an excess of oxygen. The units are expressed in kJ/mol.

As the definition specifically refers to 1 mole, each substance has only one heat of combustion. If we burn more than or less than one mole, the heat produced is not the heat of combustion until we adjust it back to 1 mole.

Remember that 1 mole can also be expressed as its molecular or atomic mass in grams, or 22.4 litres at s.t.p. for a gas.

Writing equations for heats of combustion

The substance undergoing combustion is written on the left-hand side of the equation as it is a reactant in the reaction. As we are writing an equation for the heat of combustion, 1 mole of the substance must be burned.

H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O
C₂H₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 2H₂O
C₂H₅OH + 3O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

The bomb calorimeter

We can accurately measure the heat of combustion of a substance using a piece of apparatus called the bomb calorimeter. It contains a crucible to hold the substance being burned, wires that can be electrically heated to ignite the sample, an inlet tube to provide an excess of oxygen and a screw-on lid. The bomb is then placed in a container of water that contains a thermometer to measure the heat change in the water and a stirrer so that the heat is evenly distributed throughout the water.

By knowing the amount of substance burned, the initial and final temperatures of the water, the specific heat capacity of the water, the heat capacity of the calorimeter and the mass of the water, it is possible to measure the heat of combustion of the substance.

Course note: the full bomb-calorimeter calculation is not examined here, but how the apparatus works is examinable.

Kilogram calorific value

KILOGRAM CALORIFIC VALUE
Defn – The heat that is produced when 1 kg of a fuel is burned in an excess of oxygen. Units: kJ/kg.

If given the heat of combustion of a substance, we can easily find the kilogram calorific value and vice versa.

Example : if the heat of combustion of ethane is −1560 kJ/mol, the kilogram calorific value is −52,000 kJ/kg.

Heat of formation

HEAT OF FORMATION
Defn – The heat change that occurs when 1 mole of a substance is formed from its elements in their standard states. Units: kJ/mol.

The standard state of an element or compound is its form at 25 °C and 1 atm. Carbon, sodium and iron are monatomic solids in their standard states; hydrogen, oxygen and nitrogen are diatomic gases in their standard states.

Writing equations for heats of formation

The substance to be formed is written on the right-hand side of the equation, as it is the only product. Because it is a heat of formation equation, 1 mole must be formed.

2C(s) + 2H₂(g) → C₂H₄(g)
2C(s) + 3H₂(g) + ½O₂(g) → C₂H₅OH(l)
½N₂(g) + O₂(g) → NO₂(g)

Hess’s law

HESS’S LAW
When a reaction takes place in stages, the sum of the heat changes of the individual stages equals the heat change if the reaction were to take place in one stage.

Hess’s law is really the law of conservation of energy applied to heat changes in chemical reactions.

LAW OF CONSERVATION OF ENERGY
Defn – Energy cannot be created or destroyed but can only be converted from one form of energy into another.
  1. Write the required equation.
  2. Write the equations for the given information.
  3. Reverse / multiply equations so the substances end up on the correct sides.
  4. Cancel what you can.
  5. Add the heats of reaction to get the target value.

Heat of neutralisation

The heats of neutralisation of hydrochloric acid, nitric acid and sulfuric acid are roughly the same (about −57 kJ/mol) because essentially the same reaction is happening in all: H⁺ from the acid reacts with OH⁻ from the base to form water.

With weak acids such as ethanoic acid, the heat of neutralisation is less than −57 kJ/mol because extra heat is needed to fully dissociate the acid first.

Experiment — HCl with NaOH

A known number of moles of standard acid is mixed with a known number of moles of base in an insulated container. The initial temperatures are measured and the final steady temperature is recorded. The heat change in this experiment is small, so relatively concentrated solutions are needed to get an appreciable temperature rise.

  • Apparatus – two polystyrene cups with lids, thermometer (reading to 0.1 °C), stirrer.
  • Materials – 100 cm³ of 1 M HCl, 100 cm³ of 1 M NaOH.
  • Use a plastic / polystyrene container to reduce heat loss.

Bond energy

BOND ENERGY
Defn – the energy required to break 1 mole of covalent bonds and to separate the neutral atoms completely from each other.

When bonds are broken, energy is required (endothermic). When bonds are made, energy is released (exothermic).

In the combustion of methane, 4 C–H bonds and 2 O=O bonds are broken; then 2 C=O bonds and 4 O–H bonds are formed.

Energy profile diagrams

In an endothermic reaction, the products have more energy than the reactants, so energy is taken in from the surroundings.

In an exothermic reaction, the products have less energy than the reactants, so heat is lost to the surroundings.
