Atomic Theory 9 — Covalent, Sigma/Pi, Dative

At a glance

This section covers covalent bonding, sigma and pi bonding, pure and polar covalent bonds, dative covalent bonds, and how shape affects molecular polarity.

Sigma bond = head-on overlap Pi bond = side-on overlap Pure covalent = equal sharing Polar covalent = unequal sharing Dative bond = shared pair from one atom

Bonding overview

Covalent bonding

Covalent bond – the bond formed when atoms share a pair of electrons. The attraction between the positively charged nuclei and the shared electrons holds the atoms together.

Single, double and triple covalent bonds are all covered here. A single bond has one shared pair of electrons, a double bond has two shared pairs and a triple bond has three shared pairs.

Important note: the high-energy triple bond in N₂ makes nitrogen gas very unreactive. Examples include lightning and nitrogen-fixing bacteria as natural ways that bond can be broken.
Type of bondExampleMain idea
SingleH–Clone shared pair
DoubleO=Otwo shared pairs
TripleN≡Nthree shared pairs

Sigma and pi bonding

Covalent bonding involves the overlap of orbitals. The first bond formed between two atoms is always a sigma bond, because it comes from the greatest overlap and gives most stability.

Sigma bond – a covalent bond formed by head-on overlap of orbitals.
Pi bond – a covalent bond formed by side-on overlap of p orbitals. Pi bonds form after the sigma bond has already formed.

Examples:

  • HCl: the 1s orbital of H overlaps head-on with a p orbital of Cl to form a sigma bond.
  • Cl₂: a head-on p–p overlap forms a sigma bond.
  • O₂: one sigma bond and one pi bond.
  • N₂: one sigma bond and two pi bonds.

Types of covalent bonding

Pure covalent bond – the bonding electrons are shared equally between the atoms.

Examples include H₂, O₂, F₂ and Cl₂.

Polar covalent bond – the bonding electrons are shared unequally because one atom has a greater attraction for the shared pair.

Examples include HCl and H₂O. In HCl, chlorine is δ− and hydrogen is δ+. In water, oxygen is δ− and each hydrogen is δ+.

Bond typeHow electrons are sharedExamples
Pure covalentequallyH₂, O₂, Cl₂
Polar covalentunequallyHCl, H₂O
Dative covalentshared pair from one atom onlyNH₄⁺, H₃O⁺

Dative covalent bonding and molecular polarity

Dative covalent bond – a covalent bond where both electrons in the shared pair are donated by one atom.

A standard syllabus example is the ammonium ion, NH₄⁺. Ammonia donates a lone pair to H⁺. Another common example is H₃O⁺.

Not all molecules with polar bonds are polar molecules. The shape must be considered as well. Water is V-shaped and therefore polar. Molecules such as CO₂, BF₃ and CCl₄ can have polar bonds but still be non-polar overall because the dipoles cancel in a symmetrical arrangement.

Key phrase to learn: a molecule with polar bonds can still be non-polar if the centres of positive and negative charge coincide or the dipole moments cancel.

General properties of covalent compounds

  • They are often gases or liquids at room temperature.
  • They usually have low melting and boiling points because the intermolecular forces are weak.
  • Pure covalent compounds do not conduct electricity because they have no ions.
  • They are usually insoluble in water but soluble in non-polar solvents.

Practice prompts

Exam-style prompt

Define electronegativity and account for the trend down Group 17.

Show answer
  • Electronegativity: a measure of the attraction of an atom in a molecule for the shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond.
  • Trend down Group 17: electronegativity decreases.
  • Reason: more shells and more shielding mean the nucleus attracts the shared pair less strongly.

Exam-style prompt

Would you expect H₂S to be linear or non-linear in shape? Justify your answer.

Show answer
  • Shape: non-linear / bent.
  • Reason: sulfur has two bonding pairs and two lone pairs.
  • Effect: lone-pair repulsion pushes the bond pairs down, so the molecule is not linear.

Exam-style prompt

Why does water have a much higher boiling point than hydrogen sulfide?

Show answer
  • Water: hydrogen bonding between molecules.
  • H₂S: no hydrogen bonding; only weaker dipole–dipole / van der Waals forces.
  • Therefore: more energy is needed to separate water molecules.

Exam-style prompt

Explain how a molecule with polar bonds can still be non-polar overall.

Show answer

Answer: if the molecule is symmetrical, the bond dipoles cancel. The centres of positive and negative charge coincide, so there is no overall dipole moment.
