Organic Chemistry 10

Organic Chemistry 10 — Preparation of Benzoic Acid

At a glance

Reaction overview

Benzyl alcohol is oxidised first to an aldehyde and then to the carboxylic acid under alkaline manganate(VII) conditions.

Notes

Theory behind the experiment

An alcohol is oxidised in alkaline conditions to an aldehyde and then further oxidised to a carboxylic acid. The alcohol used is phenyl methanol (benzyl alcohol). The oxidising agent is potassium manganate(VII) and alkaline conditions are provided by sodium carbonate.

Why excess KMnO₄ is used: excess oxidising agent is needed to ensure complete oxidation to benzoic acid; otherwise phenyl methanal would remain.

During the oxidation, manganate(VII) is reduced to a brown precipitate of MnO₂.

Role of the later reagents

  • Hydrochloric acid is added to precipitate the benzoic acid crystals, neutralise sodium carbonate / potassium hydroxide, and protonate the benzoate ion.
  • Sodium sulfite solution is used to reduce the brown MnO₂ impurity to colourless Mn²⁺ so the crystals are not contaminated.

Method flow

  1. Add phenyl methanol to the flask.
  2. Add excess saturated KMnO₄ and sodium carbonate.
  3. Warm / heat to complete oxidation.
  4. Add sodium sulfite in a water bath to clear the brown manganese dioxide impurity.
  5. Add hydrochloric acid, then cool to crystallise benzoic acid.
  6. Isolate by filtration and purify by recrystallisation.
  7. Assess purity by melting point.

Experiment ideas from the question set

  • colour change during oxidation
  • identifying the reagents added to isolate benzoic acid
  • observations on cooling and crystallisation
  • change in oxidation number of manganese
  • recrystallisation and melting-point range
  • percentage-yield calculation from phenyl methanol
Likely observations collected in one place: purple manganate(VII) is consumed; brown MnO₂ appears; sulfite clears the brown colour; acidification and cooling produce benzoic-acid crystals.
