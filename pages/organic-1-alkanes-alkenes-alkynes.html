Organic Chemistry 1 — Alkanes, Alkenes, Alkynes

At a glance

Carbon geometry quick view

Unsaturation tests

Notes

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon based compounds. Industrially, organic chemistry is extremely important. Each year it produces billions of Euro as it is necessary for the production of pharmaceuticals, petrol, diesel, plastics, paints and cosmetics, to name just a few.

To begin, we will confine our study to three important homologous series or “families” of organic compounds, namely alkanes, alkenes and alkynes.

Remember that carbon is in group 4 in the periodic table and so has a valency of four, so it can form four bonds in total. These bonds may comprise of:

  • Four single bonds – saturated. (4 bonding pairs; no lone pairs – tetrahedral)
  • One double bond and two single bonds – unsaturated. (3 bonding pairs; no lone pairs – planar)
  • One triple bond and one single bond – unsaturated. (2 bonding pairs; no lone pairs – planar)
SATURATED
Defn – contains only single bonds around carbon atoms.
UNSATURATED
Defn – contains a multiple bond between two carbon atoms.
HYDROCARBON
Defn – a hydrocarbon is a compound that consists of the elements carbon and hydrogen only.
ALIPHATIC HYDROCARBONS
Defn – are straight or branched-chained hydrocarbon molecules.
AROMATIC HYDROCARBONS
Defn – are hydrocarbons that contain a benzene ring in their structure.

Sources of hydrocarbons

  • Coal – a black solid that contains mainly carbon. Burning coal causes air pollution.
  • Natural gas (methane) – produced when dead organic material is converted into hydrocarbon molecules over millions of years. It is found in slurry pits, coal mines, intestinal bacteria of cows and sheep and rubbish dumps. The natural gas from the Kinsale gas fields is made up of 95% methane. Methane contributes to the greenhouse effect.
  • Petroleum (crude oil) – consists of many aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbons. Crude oil is used to make drugs, plastics, cosmetics, petrol, diesel and paints.

Hydrocarbons are fuels — they release energy when burned in oxygen.

Organic compound + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Alkanes

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

  • All members of the alkanes have single bonds around / between the carbon atoms, i.e. they are saturated compounds.
  • The names of alkane molecules end in –ane, e.g. methane, ethane, propane.
  • Lower members are gases; middle members are liquids; higher members are waxy solids.
  • Alkane molecules are held together by weak van der Waals forces of attraction.
  • Each successive member of the alkanes differs by CH₂.
  • Alkanes are insoluble in water as they are non-polar molecules and do not form hydrogen bonds with water.
  • Because they are saturated they are unreactive; they do not decolourise bromine water or dilute potassium manganate(VII).
  • Alkanes typically undergo free radical substitution reactions.
Typical condition for halogen substitution: reagent Cl₂, condition u.v. light

Substituted alkanes

  • The longest carbon chain is the parent alkane.
  • Name from the end that gives the lowest number to the attached group.
  • Substituted groups are named and numbered alphabetically.
  • Use di-, tri-, tetra- if more than one of the same group is present.
Very important note – if you put an alkyl group on the first carbon of a molecule it is simply an extension of the chain.

Isomers

Note that butane (C₄H₁₀) has two structural isomers: butane and 2-methylpropane. It also gives the answer that pentane has three isomers: pentane, 2-methylbutane and 2,2-dimethylpropane.

Alkenes

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ

  • All members of the alkenes contain one double bond between two of the carbon atoms, i.e. they are unsaturated.
  • The names of the alkenes end in –ene.
  • Each successive member differs by CH₂.
  • Alkenes are insoluble in water but soluble in non-polar solvents.
  • Because they are unsaturated they are reactive and will decolourise bromine water and dilute potassium manganate(VII).
  • Alkenes typically undergo ionic addition reactions. The double bond becomes single and one main product is produced.
  • The first member of the alkenes is ethene because you must have two carbons to have a double bond.
But-1-ene and but-2-ene are structural isomers of each other. The number in the middle of the name gives the position of the double bond.

Substituted alkenes

When naming substituted alkene molecules, the same rules apply as before, but the direction must first be chosen so that the double bond gets the lowest possible number.

Example : draw the structural formula of 1,2-dibromo-3-methylbut-1-ene.

Alkynes

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

  • All members of the alkynes have a triple bond between two carbon atoms and are unsaturated.
  • The names of the alkynes end in –yne, e.g. ethyne, propyne.
  • Ethyne is a gas. Van der Waals forces exist between the molecules.
  • Each successive member differs by CH₂.
  • Alkynes are insoluble in water but soluble in non-polar solvents such as chloroform or cyclohexane.
  • They decolourise bromine water and dilute potassium manganate(VII), proving they are unsaturated.
  • Alkynes typically undergo ionic addition reactions.

The common name for ethyne is acetylene. It is used for cutting and welding metals in the form of oxyacetylene because when ethyne is burned in oxygen it reaches temperatures of over 3000 °C.

Aromatic and cyclic hydrocarbons

Benzene was discovered by Faraday in the early 1800s. It is a highly unsaturated compound but was unexpectedly found to be unreactive.

Why? Benzene does not have ordinary single and double bonds between the carbons as was originally thought. The carbon–carbon bond length in benzene is intermediate between a single and a double bond. This makes benzene stable and unreactive.

  • Benzene is insoluble in water.
  • The molecule is hexagonal planar and all bond angles are 120°.
  • Benzene is carcinogenic and toxic.
  • Benzene does not decolourise bromine water or dilute potassium manganate(VII).

Support diagram — benzene ring

Other aromatic and cyclic hydrocarbons that you may come across are methylbenzene (toluene), phenylethene (styrene), cyclohexane and ethylbenzene.

Methylbenzene is used in preference to benzene in the lab as benzene is toxic and carcinogenic. Methylbenzene is also a good organic solvent.

Answer outlines for the revision exercises above

Functional groups and definitions

Draw the functional group of (a) alkenes and (b) alkynes. Explain the terms homologous series, functional group and isomers.

Show answer outline
  • Alkene functional group: C=C.
  • Alkyne functional group: C≡C.
  • Homologous series: a family of compounds with the same functional group and similar chemical properties in which successive members differ by CH2.
  • Functional group: the atom or group of atoms responsible for the characteristic reactions of a compound.
  • Isomers: compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural formulae.
Reaction types

What type of reactions do alkanes and alkenes typically undergo? How many products would you usually expect?

Show answer outline
  • Alkanes: free-radical substitution.
  • Products: often more than one product because substitution can continue.
  • Alkenes: ionic addition / electrophilic addition.
  • Products: usually one main product from addition across the double bond.
Solubility and bonding

Would propane be soluble in water? Explain. Name the intermolecular forces between alkane molecules.

Show answer outline
  • Propane: insoluble in water.
  • Reason: propane is non-polar and cannot hydrogen-bond with water.
  • Intermolecular forces in alkanes: van der Waals / London dispersion forces.
Tetrahedral and planar carbons

How many tetrahedral and planar carbons are in propane and 2-methylbut-1-ene?

Show answer outline
  • Propane: 3 tetrahedral, 0 planar.
  • 2-methylbut-1-ene: 3 tetrahedral, 2 planar.
  • Carbons in a C=C bond are planar; carbons with four single bonds are tetrahedral.
Observations and equations

State what you would observe if propene is reacted with bromine water and potassium manganate(VII). Write balanced equations for the combustion of ethane and propene.

Show answer outline
  • Bromine water: decolourises.
  • Acidified / dilute KMnO4: purple decolourises.
  • What it proves: propene is unsaturated.
  • Ethane: 2C2H6 + 7O2 → 4CO2 + 6H2O.
  • Propene: 2C3H6 + 9O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O.
