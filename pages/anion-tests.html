Anion Tests

Anion Tests

At a glance

Anion test flow

Notes

In this section of the course you may be asked mini-experiments to test for the presence of various ions in aqueous solution. You must know the reagent used, the observation before and after, the name and formula of any precipitate, and a balanced equation where required.

Precipitation reaction
Defn – a reaction in which an insoluble product is produced from soluble reactants.

Sulfate ion, SO₄²⁻

Add barium chloride solution: a white precipitate of barium sulfate forms.

Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄(s)

Add dilute HCl to distinguish: no change, because barium sulfate is insoluble in dilute acid.

Sulfite ion, SO₃²⁻

Add barium chloride solution: a white precipitate of barium sulfite forms.

Ba²⁺ + SO₃²⁻ → BaSO₃(s)

Add dilute HCl: the white precipitate disappears and sulfur dioxide gas is given off.

BaSO₃ + 2HCl → BaCl₂ + H₂O + SO₂↑

Carbonate and hydrogencarbonate ions

Add dilute acid. If carbonate or hydrogencarbonate is present, carbon dioxide is given off with fizzing / effervescence.

CO₃²⁻ + 2H⁺ → CO₂ + H₂O
HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ → CO₂ + H₂O

Test the carbon dioxide with limewater: it turns milky due to a white precipitate of calcium carbonate.

Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O

To distinguish carbonate from hydrogencarbonate, add magnesium sulfate solution. A white precipitate indicates carbonate; no precipitate indicates hydrogencarbonate. If the hydrogencarbonate solution is boiled, a white precipitate appears because hydrogencarbonates decompose on heating.

Nitrate ion, NO₃⁻ — brown ring test

Nitrates are soluble, so there is no precipitate test. Instead use the brown ring test with freshly made iron(II) sulfate solution and concentrated sulfuric acid.

  1. Add the nitrate solution to a test tube with water.
  2. Add fresh FeSO₄ solution.
  3. Carefully pour concentrated H₂SO₄ down the side of the tube.
  4. A brown ring at the junction indicates nitrate.

Chloride ion, Cl⁻

Add silver nitrate solution to the acidified sample (with nitric acid). An immediate white precipitate of silver chloride proves chloride ion.

Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl(s)

Confirm by adding dilute ammonia: silver chloride dissolves.

Phosphate ion, PO₄³⁻

Add ammonium molybdate solution and then a few drops of concentrated nitric acid. Heat gently. A yellow precipitate of ammonium phosphomolybdate forms.

Exam note: the handout says no heat, no reaction.

Past-paper pattern

Practice includes many Leaving Cert questions from 1989 to 2016. The most repeated asks are: confirm nitrate, confirm sulfite, test for chloride, distinguish sulfate and sulfite, and use the 2009 “clock glass” identification question to combine several tests in sequence.

Quick answer outlines

Sulfate and sulfite

How would you distinguish sulfate ions from sulfite ions in solution?

Show answer outline
  • Add BaCl2 to both: each gives a white precipitate.
  • Add dilute HCl to the precipitate.
  • Sulfate: BaSO4 remains.
  • Sulfite: BaSO3 dissolves with SO2 gas.
Carbonate and hydrogencarbonate

How would you distinguish CO32− from HCO3−?

Show answer outline
  • Both give CO2 with dilute acid.
  • Add MgSO4.
  • Carbonate: immediate white precipitate.
  • Hydrogencarbonate: no precipitate at first; precipitate appears on boiling.
Nitrate

Describe the brown ring test for nitrate ions.

Show answer outline
  • Add fresh FeSO4 solution.
  • Carefully pour concentrated H2SO4 down the side of the tube.
  • Positive result: brown ring at the junction of the two layers.
Chloride

How do you test for chloride ions and how do you confirm the result?

Show answer outline
  • Add AgNO3 and a few drops of concentrated HNO3.
  • Observation: white precipitate of AgCl.
  • Confirm: add dilute NH3; the precipitate dissolves.
Phosphate

How do you test for phosphate ions?

Show answer outline
  • Add ammonium molybdate and a few drops of concentrated HNO3.
  • Heat gently.
  • Observation: yellow precipitate.
