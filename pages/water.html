Hardness in water

In some parts of Ireland when soap is put into water, a lather is produced, however in other parts of Ireland instead of a lather, a scum is produced. This 'scum' is caused by hardness present in the water supply. This hardness is caused by Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions.

The Ca²⁺ (or Mg²⁺) ions that cause the hardness in the water combine with soap to form the scum and sodium ions (these sodium ions do not cause hardness). A lather will only be produced when all of the calcium ions are gone and so a lot of soap needs to be used.

Types of hardness

1. Temporary hardness – caused by the presence of calcium hydrogencarbonate. This type of hardness enters the water when carbonic acid (acid rain) reacts with limestone in the ground. This type of hardness can be removed by boiling. The calcium hydrogencarbonate that causes the temporary hardness is soluble in water but when heated it forms insoluble calcium carbonate which is responsible for the 'fur' or 'scale' that coats the elements of kettles, washing machines, dishwashers or can build up and block pipes.
Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O + CO₂
soluble → insoluble (scale)
2. Permanent hardness – caused by the presence of calcium sulfate, CaSO₄, or magnesium sulfate, MgSO₄. This type of hardness cannot be removed by boiling, as sulfates do not decompose on heating.

Methods of removing both types of hardness

  1. Distillation – the water is boiled off through a Liebig condenser and the hydrogencarbonates and sulfates remain in the flask. This is too expensive on a large scale.
  2. Addition of washing soda crystals, Na₂CO₃ – the carbonate ions in the washing soda crystals react with the calcium ions that cause the hardness (both temporary and permanent) and thus remove them.
  3. Ion exchange resins –
    • (a) One type of resin can be represented by Na₂R. When the hard water passes through these resins, the calcium ions that cause the hardness are 'swapped' with the sodium ions in the resin, thereby removing the cause of hardness as the calcium ions remain in the resin. Eventually the resin becomes full of calcium ions and so must be soaked in a concentrated solution of sodium chloride which will replace the calcium ions with sodium ions.
    • (b) Another type of resin will remove all of the ions in the water (not just the calcium/magnesium ions) to produce 'deionised' water. This involves two resins, a cation exchange resin and an anion exchange resin. The cation exchange resin contains H⁺ ions which will swap with any positive ions in the water. The anion exchange resin contains OH⁻ ions which will swap with any negative ions in the water. The H⁺ ions combine with the OH⁻ ions to produce water.

Advantages of hard water

  1. Tastes better.
  2. Contains calcium, which is good for bones and teeth.
  3. Good for the brewing industry (better beer!).

Disadvantages of hard water

  1. 'Dirty' looking scum produced with soap.
  2. Wastes soap.
  3. Produces 'scale' that can block pipes and make the heating elements useless.

Experiment — Estimation of total hardness in a water sample using EDTA

Theory: Hardness in water is caused by the presence of calcium and magnesium hydrogencarbonates or sulfates. This hardness can be removed by titrating the hard water sample against a standard solution of ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA). We use the disodium salt of the acid. When the disodium salt reacts with the calcium and magnesium ions a complex is formed, thus removing the hardness. EDTA and calcium ions react in a 1:1 ratio.
M²⁺ + H₂Y²⁻ → MY²⁻ + 2H⁺
Key points for the titration:
  • The indicator is Eriochrome Black T (or Solochrome Black T). This is a black powder.
  • When this indicator is added to a water sample containing hardness a wine/red colour is observed. When all the hardness has been removed a blue colour is observed. So the colour change at the end point is wine/red → blue.
  • The end point of this titration is difficult to detect and so must be carried out at a particular pH. A buffer solution of pH 10 is added to the water sample in the conical flask to give the required pH.
  • EDTA is stored in a plastic container as it has the ability to extract metal ions from glass if left in a glass container for too long.
  • Even though the hardness is caused by the presence of calcium and magnesium hydrogencarbonates or sulfates, for simplicity we attribute all hardness as being caused by calcium carbonate, CaCO₃, which has a Mr of 100.

Method

  1. The burette was washed with deionised water to clean it and then with some of the 0.01 M EDTA solution to prevent dilution of the solution.
  2. The burette was then filled using a funnel observing the usual precautions.
  3. This was then clamped vertically.
  4. The pipette was washed with some deionised water to clean it and then with the water sample to prevent dilution of the sample.
  5. 25 cm³ of the hard water sample were pipetted into the conical flask by touching the tip of the pipette off the inside of the conical flask.
  6. 1 cm³ of the buffer solution of pH 10 was added into the conical flask.
  7. A pinch of Eriochrome Black T was then added into the conical flask also.
  8. The solution in the conical flask is now a wine/red colour due to hardness present.
  9. The titration was carried out in the usual manner.
  10. The endpoint of the titration occurred when the wine/red solution in the conical flask turned blue. This was repeated two more times.

Typical results and calculation

TitrationTitre (cm³)
Rough15.4
1st15.2
2nd15.3

Water sample: V₁ = 25 cm³, M₁ = x, n₁ = 1

  2. Moles of CaCO₃ = 0.0001525 mol (1:1 ratio)
  3. Molarity = 0.0001525 / (25/1000) = 0.0061 mol L⁻¹
  4. Grams per litre = 0.0061 × 100 = 0.61 g L⁻¹
  5. ppm = 0.61 × 1000 = 610 ppm total hardness

Pollution

Organic waste, when in a water supply, puts a demand on the dissolved oxygen content of the water. This dissolved oxygen is necessary for aquatic life, fish and plants alike. If organic waste is in the water, it needs to be decomposed. Bacteria and other organisms that are in the water carry out this decomposition. If too much waste is in the water then a lot of bacteria will also be present as there is a lot of food for the bacteria. These organisms use up the dissolved oxygen to convert the waste into carbon dioxide and water and will reduce the oxygen content, so fish may die. The way in which oxygen is absorbed into the water cannot keep up with the demand for the oxygen and so the water becomes polluted.

Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD)

BOD – the amount of dissolved oxygen consumed by biochemical action when a sample of water is kept in the dark for 5 days at 20 °C.
  1. Two samples of water are taken in two containers. Wet the inside of the containers as this prevents air bubbles. The container should be filled (A) under water to stop atmospheric oxygen from being trapped and (B) filled to the brim so that no air is found between the water and the stopper.
  2. One of the water samples has its dissolved oxygen content determined using the Winkler method. This is expressed in ppm of oxygen.
  3. The other sample of water is stored in the dark at 20 °C for five days. Keeping the sample in the dark is important to prevent possible oxygen production by photosynthesis. 20 °C is needed to validate results so that conditions are kept constant. 5 days allows enough time for consumption of oxygen to occur.
  4. After the five day period is up, the second water sample is tested using the Winkler method and again the amount of dissolved oxygen is expressed in ppm.
  5. The BOD is the difference between the two dissolved oxygen levels as this indicates the amount of oxygen consumed by biochemical action.
Important: BOD is a RATE and so its value may be a lot greater or less than the amount of dissolved oxygen in the samples as the rate at which the dissolved oxygen is used up is independent of the actual amount of dissolved oxygen in the water. For example you can have a BOD of 320 ppm while the maximum D.O. concentration in fresh water is 9 ppm.

The higher the BOD the more polluted the water is as it means that many bacteria are present using up the oxygen, hence there must be a lot of waste in the water for the bacteria to decompose.

Dilution: The water samples are usually diluted to a known volume using well-oxygenated water to ensure that there is enough oxygen present for the five-day period of the test. Otherwise, all of the oxygen may be used up before the five-day period and a valid BOD would not be obtained. Adding extra oxygen does not affect the rate at which micro-organisms use it up.

Eutrophication

Excessive amounts of nitrates and phosphates in a water supply can cause eutrophication. Nitrogen and phosphorus can cause a huge increase in the amount of certain plant life i.e. algae. This is known as 'algal bloom'. These algae do not live for long and so when they die they are decomposed by large amounts of bacteria that put a large demand (use up) on the dissolved oxygen. One way that eutrophication can occur is when artificial fertilisers leach into water supplies, or if sewage from houses or farms enters water supplies.

'Heavy metal' pollution

Metals with high atomic masses can enter water supplies if industrial sewage enters a water supply or old batteries are dumped illegally. These metals include lead(II), Pb²⁺, mercury(II), Hg²⁺, and cadmium(II), Cd²⁺. These metals can build up in our bodies and cause damage i.e. birth defects and even death. However, most of these metals are excreted by our bodies in our urine and so do not get the chance to build up to toxic levels.

The impacts of heavy metal pollution on living organisms are very serious. Heavy metals are bio-accumulative, toxic at high concentrations, have neurological impacts, and some are carcinogenic. They can also interfere with chemical processes by poisoning chemical catalysts and can impact on biochemical processes by interfering with enzyme action. There are hence serious environmental, economic and social impacts associated with heavy metal pollution.

Removal: Heavy metal ions are usually removed by precipitation. For example, lead ions are reacted with chloride ions to form insoluble lead chloride.

Experiment — Estimation of dissolved oxygen using the Winkler method

Theory: A solution of manganese(II) sulfate and alkaline potassium iodide (KOH/KI) are added to the bottom of the water sample. These reagents must be concentrated to ensure excess so that (i) the maximum amount of iodine possible is liberated (the oxygen is the limiting reactant) and (ii) to minimise the amount of water displaced.

Chemistry of the Winkler method

  1. The Mn²⁺ ions react with the OH⁻ ions from the alkali to form a white precipitate of manganese(II) hydroxide, Mn(OH)₂.
  2. This white precipitate rapidly turns brown in the presence of the dissolved oxygen in the water sample. The brown precipitate is due to the formation of manganese(III) hydroxide, Mn(OH)₃. (If the precipitate stays white then there is no oxygen in the sample.)
  3. Some concentrated sulfuric acid is now added to the brown precipitate so that the iodide ions in the potassium iodide react with the manganese(III) hydroxide to liberate iodine.
  4. The amount of iodine liberated can then be calculated by titrating it against a standard sodium thiosulfate solution.
I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻
Key ratio: 1 mole of O₂ produces 2 moles of I₂. (Or: 4 moles of S₂O₃²⁻ per 1 mole of O₂.)

Method

  1. The clear glass bottle to contain the sample was washed out with water to clean it and when wet prevents air bubbles.
  2. The bottle was then totally submerged in the water to be tested and was filled to the top.
  3. 1 cm³ of manganese(II) sulfate was then added deep into the bottle to ensure that none of the Winkler reagents are lost due to overflow, then about the same volume of alkaline potassium iodide was added deep into the bottle also.
  4. Note the brown precipitate appearing.
  5. The bottle was then stoppered and some of the water overflowed.
  6. The bottle was shaken thoroughly for about 1 minute and then the bottle was left standing until the precipitate settled. There was about 5 cm of clear liquid below the stopper.
  7. 1 cm³ of concentrated sulfuric acid was then added using a graduated dropper. The bottle was then restoppered carefully and shaken to dissolve the brown precipitate. This procedure was then repeated until the precipitate dissolved.
  8. The solution was then the red/brown colour of iodine.
  9. 0.02 M sodium thiosulfate was placed in the burette observing the usual precautions.
  10. 100 cm³ of the water sample were placed in the conical flask observing the usual precautions.
  11. The two solutions were titrated until the solution in the conical flask was straw yellow. At this point some starch indicator was added and the solution went blue/black in colour.
  12. The titration was continued until the solution in the conical flask went from blue/black to colourless. This colour change indicates the end-point of the titration.

TitrationTitre (cm³)
Rough5.9
1st5.7
2nd5.6
Average5.65

Water sample: V₁ = 100 cm³
Sodium thiosulfate: V₂ = 5.65 cm³, M₂ = 0.02 M

  1. Moles of Na₂S₂O₃ = 0.02 × 5.65/1000 = 0.000113 mol
  2. Moles of I₂ = 0.000113 / 2 = 0.0000565 mol
  3. Moles of O₂ = 0.0000565 / 2 = 0.00002825 mol (since 1 mol O₂ → 2 mol I₂)
  4. Moles of O₂ per litre = 0.00002825 × (1000/100) = 0.0002825 mol L⁻¹
  5. Grams per litre = 0.0002825 × 32 = 0.00904 g L⁻¹
  6. ppm = 0.00904 × 1000 = 9.04 ppm dissolved oxygen
Important: The Winkler method does not work for tap water as it has been chlorinated (or any water that has been chlorinated). This is because chlorine is an oxidising agent and would cause more iodine to be liberated than should and this extra iodine would give an inaccurate titre result hence an inaccurate dissolved oxygen result.

Water treatment — preparation of water for drinking

  1. Screening – the water is passed through graded screens to remove debris.
  2. Flocculation – a flocculating agent such as aluminium sulfate, Al₂(SO₄)₃, is added to cause any suspended particles to coagulate together so that they can be removed.
  3. Settlement (sedimentation) – the water passes into settlement tanks so that the coagulated suspended particles can settle on the bottom of the tank.
  4. Filtration – the water passes through filter beds (sand and gravel) and any remaining suspended particles can be removed.
  5. Chlorination – chlorine is added in small quantities, usually as sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) to sterilise the water, as it may contain harmful bacteria. 'Free chlorine' is an oxidising agent and will oxidise the bacterial enzymes.
  6. Fluoridation – hexafluorosilicic acid is added (1 ppm) to prevent tooth decay.
  7. pH adjustment – if the pH of the water is too high then dilute sulfuric acid is added. If the pH is too low then calcium hydroxide is added. The pH of drinking water should be in the range of 7–9. An acidic pH would lead to corrosion of pipes.

Sewage treatment

Sewage is used to describe the waste that comes from houses, factories, farms, businesses etc. It may contain organic waste and inorganic waste. If raw sewage were allowed into rivers or lakes the water would become very polluted and so must be treated first. Its objective is to produce an environmentally safe fluid waste stream (or treated effluent) and a solid waste (or treated sludge) suitable for disposal or reuse (usually as farm fertiliser).

Primary treatment (mainly physical)

  • The sewage is screened to remove large debris.
  • It then passes through 'grit-traps' to remove smaller debris.
  • This is then passed into large settlement tanks to allow suspended particles to settle as sludge at the bottom of the tanks (sedimentation).
  • Grease and oil, which can be floating on the water, can be recovered for saponification.
  • The liquid on top moves on to the secondary stage.

Secondary treatment (mainly biological)

  • The liquid is passed into large aeration tanks that continuously churn the water around.
  • Micro-organisms in the tank break down the organic and suspended particles to harmless substances.
  • The churning increases the dissolved oxygen content of the water.
  • The water then passes into a settlement tank and some of the sludge is removed and can be used as a fertiliser or anaerobically oxidised by bacteria to produce methane (fuel).
  • After this stage, about 95% of the original sewage is removed and so can be let into rivers or lakes.
  • However it may contain dissolved chemicals containing nitrogen and phosphorus. These can be removed by tertiary treatment.

Tertiary treatment (mainly chemical)

  • The water contains dissolved nitrates from organic waste and dissolved phosphates from washing powders.
  • If left in the water these substances can cause eutrophication.
  • The removal of nitrogen is effected through the biological oxidation of nitrogen from ammonia to nitrate (nitrification), followed by denitrification, the reduction of nitrate to nitrogen gas. Nitrogen gas is released to the atmosphere and thus removed from the water.
  • Phosphates are removed by precipitation. Calcium and/or aluminium ions are added and the phosphates precipitate out of solution and are allowed to settle before the final effluent is let into waterways. The nutrient rich sludge can be used as fertiliser.

Instrumental methods of analysis

  1. Use of a pH meter – a pH meter is used in water treatment plants to check the pH of the water. This piece of apparatus is sensitive to hydrogen ion or hydroxide ion concentrations. The pH of the water can be adjusted as discussed before.
  2. Atomic Absorption Spectrometry (AAS) – each element has its own unique absorption spectrum. This can be used to identify and measure the concentrations of heavy metals present in the water.
  3. Colorimetry – white light is passed through a coloured solution. Only a certain amount of light gets through. This light is then passed through a photocell that converts light energy into electrical energy. The current of electricity is then shown on a meter. The amount of light that passes through a coloured solution is dependent on the concentration of the solution so it is possible to calculate the concentration of the solution.

Suspended and dissolved solids

Suspended solids – INSOLUBLE substances that are 'floating' in the water and can be removed by filtering.
Dissolved solids – SOLUBLE substances in solution in the water and can be measured by evaporating off the water leaving the dissolved solids behind.

Experiment — Determination of total suspended and dissolved solids by filtration and evaporation

(I) Measurement of total suspended solids by filtration

  1. Fill a volumetric flask with the water sample to have a known volume of water e.g. 300 cm³.
  2. The mass of the filter paper was found on the electronic balance. This mass was noted.
  3. The 300 cm³ of water were then poured through the weighed filter paper. This filtered water was kept for the next part of the experiment.
  4. The filter paper was allowed to dry fully and the dry filter paper was reweighed.
Result: The mass of the original filter paper was subtracted from the mass of the dried filter paper to find the mass of the suspended solids. This is the grams of suspended solids per 300 cm³. Adjust to find the grams per litre. Multiply the grams by 1000 to find the mg/L. This answer is the mass of suspended solids in ppm.

(II) Measurement of total dissolved solids by evaporation

  1. The mass of a clean dry beaker was recorded.
  2. Using the graduated cylinder a known volume of filtered water was poured into the beaker e.g. 200 cm³.
  3. The beaker with the filtered water was placed on the heating mantle and heated gently until all of the water had evaporated off.
  4. The beaker was allowed to cool and then was weighed. The weight was noted.
Result: The mass of the beaker (original) was subtracted from the mass of the beaker after evaporation to find the mass of the dissolved solids. This is grams per 200 cm³. Adjust to find the mass of the dissolved solids per litre. Multiply these grams by 1000 to find the mg/L. This then is the ppm of dissolved solids.

(III) Measurement of the pH

This is done using either a pH meter or using pH paper.

Experiment — Colorimetric experiment to estimate free chlorine in swimming pool water using a comparator

Principle: The intensity of the colour of a solution is proportional to its concentration.
Free chlorine – the chlorine in chloric(I) acid and hypochlorite ions is known as 'free chlorine'. Pools are sterilised with chlorine compounds which produce chloric(I) acid, HClO, when they dissolve in the water. Sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) or calcium hypochlorite (Ca(ClO)₂) are two such compounds. These chlorine compounds kill bacteria by oxidising the enzymes of the bacteria.

In this experiment we use a Hach test kit. This is a coloured disk with different shades of red on the disk. A chemical called DPD is added to the water sample. If free chlorine is present in the water then it will turn red. The degree of 'redness' depends on the concentration of free chlorine that is present in the water.

Method

  1. Deionised water is placed into one of the viewing tubes to a level just below the 'frosted' part of the glass. This was stoppered. This tube is used as a reference tube.
  2. The reference tube was placed into the comparator box to the left.
  3. The same volume of swimming pool water was placed into another viewing tube and one of the sachets of chlorine-free DPD was added to this. The tube was then stoppered and shaken to mix. Note the red/pink colour of the water sample now.
  4. This tube was then placed into the other opening of the comparator box on the right.
  5. The coloured disk was placed into the comparator box and the door was closed.
  6. The comparator box was then held up to a light source and the two 'windows' in the front of the box were looked through.
  7. The disk was then rotated until the colours in the two 'windows' matched.
  8. The number on the disk was then read. This number gives the estimated concentration of free chlorine in the water in ppm.
