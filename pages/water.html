Hardness in water

Hard water – water that does not form a lather easily with soap.

Hardness is mainly due to Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions. These react with soap to form scum, so extra soap is needed before lather appears.

Temporary hardness – hardness caused by calcium / magnesium hydrogencarbonates and removable by boiling.
Permanent hardness – hardness caused by calcium / magnesium sulfates and not removable by boiling.
Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O

The insoluble calcium carbonate formed on boiling explains limescale in kettles and pipes.

Methods of removing hardness

  • Boiling removes temporary hardness only.
  • Ion exchange / deionising resins remove both temporary and permanent hardness.
  • Washing soda and EDTA analysis are also linked to this topic.

EDTA titration for total hardness

The chapter gives the EDTA experiment as a mandatory titration.

  • 25 cm³ hard water sample in conical flask
  • buffer solution at pH 10
  • Eriochrome Black T indicator
  • wine-red to blue endpoint
  • EDTA in burette
M²⁺ + EDTA → M–EDTA complex
Convention: hardness is usually reported in ppm as calcium carbonate, even though the actual hardness may be due to other ions.

Pollution and BOD

Organic waste increases bacterial activity and therefore increases oxygen demand in water. If oxygen is used up faster than it is replaced, fish and other aquatic life may die.

Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) – the amount of dissolved oxygen consumed by biochemical action when a sample of water is kept in the dark for 5 days at 20 °C.

A useful method is: test one sample immediately, keep the other in the dark at 20 °C for 5 days, then test again. The difference in dissolved oxygen values is the BOD.

Winkler experiment

The chapter includes the Winkler method for dissolved oxygen. Two concentrated reagents are added carefully without trapping air. A brown precipitate forms, acid is added, iodine is liberated, and the iodine is titrated with sodium thiosulfate using starch near the endpoint.

Why the sample bottle is filled fully: note that trapped air would change the dissolved oxygen result. Bottles are filled under water / to the brim to avoid atmospheric oxygen contamination.

Water treatment

  • Sedimentation – suspended matter allowed to settle.
  • Flocculation – added chemicals help fine suspended particles clump together and settle.
  • Filtration – remaining suspended particles removed through filter beds.
  • Disinfection – chlorine or related agents kill microorganisms.
  • pH adjustment / fluoridation – treated as final-stage chemical additions in this chapter.

Sewage treatment and eutrophication

This chapter distinguishes primary, secondary and tertiary treatment, and ties tertiary treatment to removal of nutrients such as phosphates / nitrates to help prevent eutrophication.

Eutrophication – the over-abundant growth of plants and algae arising from excess nutrients in water.

Suspended and dissolved solids

Suspended solids are measured by filtration and weighing the retained mass. Dissolved solids are measured by evaporating filtered water to dryness and weighing the residue. Both are often reported in ppm.

Free chlorine in swimming-pool water

This chapter includes a colorimetry / comparator experiment for free chlorine, plus a pool-quality data table linking suspended solids, dissolved solids, hardness, free chlorine and pH with whether the pool is suitable for swimming.
