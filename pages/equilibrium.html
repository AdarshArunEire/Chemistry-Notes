Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical Equilibrium

At a glance

Rates at dynamic equilibrium

Le Châtelier quick map

Notes

Not all reactions go to completion. In a reversible reaction, the products can react together to reform the reactants. At equilibrium, the amounts of reactants and products remain constant as long as the temperature stays the same, even though both reactions are still happening.

Dynamic equilibrium

Dynamic equilibrium
Defn – both the forward and reverse reactions occur at the same time. They are ongoing.

Initially only reactants are present, so the forward reaction is fast and the reverse reaction is negligible. As products build up, the reverse reaction speeds up until the forward and reverse rates become equal.

Le Châtelier’s principle in words

If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a stress, the position of equilibrium moves in the direction that opposes that stress.

Concentration changes

  • If a substance is removed, the system favours the direction that makes more of that substance.
  • If a substance is added, the system favours the direction that uses up that substance.
N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃

If more NH₃ is added, equilibrium moves left to use it up. If NH₃ is removed, equilibrium also moves right to replace it.

Temperature changes

  • If temperature is decreased, the exothermic reaction is favoured.
  • If temperature is increased, the endothermic reaction is favoured.
Remember: every equation has to be checked individually. If the forward reaction has negative ΔH, it is exothermic; the reverse is then endothermic.

If ΔH = 0, a change in temperature does not change the equilibrium position.

Pressure changes (gaseous systems only)

  • If pressure is increased, the system favours the side with the least number of moles of gas.
  • If pressure is decreased, the system favours the side with the greatest number of moles of gas.
Trap: changes in pressure do not affect systems that are not gaseous, and they do not affect gaseous equations with equal gas moles on both sides.

Kc — equilibrium constant in terms of concentration

At a given temperature there is a fixed mathematical relationship between the concentrations of reactants and products at equilibrium.

Kc
The equilibrium constant expression in terms of molar concentrations.

The concentrations of products (right-hand side) go on top; the concentrations of reactants (left-hand side) go on the bottom. Each concentration is raised to the power of its coefficient in the balanced equation.

For N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃,   Kc = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³)
For CH₄ + H₂O ⇌ CO + 3H₂,   Kc = [CO][H₂]³ / ([CH₄][H₂O])

Reading the size of Kc

  • If Kc is greater than 1, the equilibrium mixture contains relatively more products than reactants.
  • If Kc is less than 1, the equilibrium mixture contains relatively more reactants than products.
Very important: Kc is temperature dependent. If temperature changes, the value of Kc changes too.

Kc calculations

The handout gives two main types:

  1. Type 1: work out Kc from the equilibrium composition.
  2. Type 2: Kc is given and you solve for the equilibrium composition, often leading to a quadratic.

A worked example uses H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HI at two temperatures to show both methods.

Industrial / application examples

  • Steam reforming of natural gas: CH₄ + H₂O ⇌ CO + 3H₂, ΔH = +205 kJ mol⁻¹.
  • The cobalt chloride / hydrochloric acid water-equilibrium colour-change example.
  • Ethyl ethanoate formation from ethanoic acid + ethanol, used again in organic chemistry.

Past-paper focus

Practice includes many questions on defining dynamic equilibrium, stating Le Châtelier’s Principle, predicting shifts caused by changes in concentration / temperature / pressure, and calculating Kc from moles or masses.
