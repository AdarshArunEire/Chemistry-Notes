Gas Laws

Gas Laws

At a glance

Core equations

Boyle’s law: pV = constant    and    p₁V₁ = p₂V₂
Charles’s law: V/T = constant    and    V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Combined gas law: p₁V₁/T₁ = p₂V₂/T₂
Ideal gas equation: pV = nRT

Volatile-liquid experiment

The standard volatile-liquid method: vaporise a small known mass of a volatile liquid, measure the vapour volume, temperature and pressure, then use pV = nRT to find moles and hence the relative molecular mass.

Notes

States of matter and behaviour of gases

Recall that solids, liquids and gases are the three states of matter. Gases have no fixed volume; they fill any container into which they are put.

This spreading out is called diffusion. The classic ammonia / hydrochloric acid cotton-wool experiment shows diffusion, giving a white ring of ammonium chloride closer to the HCl end because ammonia diffuses faster.

Boyle’s law

Boyle’s law – when a fixed mass of gas is kept at constant temperature, its volume is inversely proportional to its pressure.

As pressure increases, the volume decreases. Boyle’s law can be expressed mathematically as pV = constant, or as p₁V₁ = p₂V₂ for two sets of conditions.

Graph point: because pV is constant, a graph of p against 1/V is a straight line. A graph of p against V shows inverse proportionality.

Charles’s law

Charles’s law – at constant pressure, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (Kelvin).

Temperature must be measured in Kelvin, not Celsius. Doubling the absolute temperature doubles the volume.

Mathematically: V/T = constant, or V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂.

Key graph idea: the graph shows that a plot of volume against temperature in Celsius extrapolates to zero volume at −273 °C (0 K).

Gay-Lussac’s law of combining volumes

Gay-Lussac’s law of combining volumes – when gases react, the volumes consumed bear a simple whole-number ratio to each other and to the gaseous products, when all volumes are measured at the same temperature and pressure.

Examples include:

  • 1 volume of nitrogen + 3 volumes of hydrogen → 2 volumes of ammonia
  • 2 volumes of sulfur dioxide + 1 volume of oxygen → 2 volumes of sulfur trioxide

Avogadro’s law

Avogadro’s law – equal volumes of gases measured under the same conditions of temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.

This explains gas-volume ratios in reactions. It also links directly to the idea that volume is proportional to the number of moles.

Molar volume – at s.t.p. one mole of any gas occupies 22.4 L (22,400 cm³).

The combined gas law and ideal gases

Combining Boyle’s and Charles’s laws gives the combined gas law:

p₁V₁ / T₁ = p₂V₂ / T₂
Ideal gas – a gas that obeys all the gas laws under all conditions of temperature and pressure.

The equation of state is also essential for exam questions:

pV = nRT
Conditions note: temperatures must be in Kelvin and the pressure / volume units must be consistent on both sides of the equation.

Standard temperature and pressure

  • Standard temperature = 0 °C = 273 K
  • Standard pressure = 101 kPa

Relative molecular mass of a volatile liquid

A major practical application is the volatile-liquid experiment. A known mass of a volatile liquid is vaporised, the vapour volume is measured, and then pV = nRT is used to calculate moles and hence relative molecular mass.

  • Measure the mass of liquid vaporised.
  • Measure the volume of the vapour.
  • Measure the temperature and atmospheric pressure.
  • Use pV = nRT to find n, then relative molecular mass = mass / moles.
Possible error sources: accuracy of the balance and gas syringe, air bubbles, and the fact that no real gas is perfectly ideal.
