Organic Chemistry 8 — Esterification, Base Hydrolysis and Soap

At a glance

Saponification summary

Cleaning action of soap

Soap has a large non-polar hydrocarbon tail and a smaller polar / ionic head. The oils on the skin dissolve in the non-polar chain, while salts / water interact with the polar functional group.

Notes

Esterification and hydrolysis

Esterification is the reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol to produce an ester and water. It is reversible, so at equilibrium it is affected by changes in temperature and concentration (not pressure, because it is not a gaseous system at room temperature and pressure).

Forward reaction: esterification    |    Reverse reaction: hydrolysis

Recall that esterification is also a condensation reaction because two different molecules react to produce a more complex molecule plus a smaller molecule such as water.

Base hydrolysis of an ester

Esters react with bases such as sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide to form two products: the alcohol and the salt of the carboxylic acid. In the manufacture of soap, this reaction is called saponification.

Soap

Soap is made when fats, which are natural esters, are heated with sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide to form the alcohol propan-1,2,3-triol and the salts of long-chain carboxylic acids. The example fat is glyceryl tristearate.

  • The alcohol produced is propan-1,2,3-triol (glycerol).
  • The salts of the long-chain carboxylic acid are sodium stearate or potassium stearate, i.e. soap.
  • Animal fat is described as saturated fat; vegetable fat as unsaturated fat.

Cleaning action of soap

Soap contains a large non-polar part, C₁₇H₃₅, and a smaller polar / ionic part. The oils from the skin dissolve in the non-polar carbon chain, while the salts and water interact with the polar functional group. This is the cleaning action of soap.

Experiment — to prepare a sample of soap

Materials

  • lard or vegetable oil
  • sodium hydroxide pellets
  • ethanol
  • saturated sodium chloride solution (brine)
  • anti-bumping granules
  • water

Method

  1. Place 5 g of lard (limiting reactant) in a pear-shaped flask.
  2. Add about 4 g of sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide (excess) and 30 cm³ of ethanol. Ethanol acts as a solvent for the lard.
  3. Add anti-bumping granules.
  4. Set up the apparatus for reflux and heat for about 30 minutes.
  5. Wash down any residue with a minimum of hot water.
  6. After reflux, allow the mixture to cool slightly.
  7. Rearrange for distillation and distil off roughly two-thirds of the ethanol solvent to help isolate the soap.
  8. Allow to cool slightly and decant into concentrated brine. Soap is insoluble in brine and precipitates out.
  9. Filter using a Büchner funnel and weighed filter paper.
  10. Wash the soap with ice-cold water to remove sodium hydroxide solution clinging to it.
  11. Dry the soap on the filter paper in a desiccator.
  12. Weigh the soap and filter paper to determine the actual yield; use moles of lard to determine theoretical yield and hence percentage yield.
Why each step matters: reflux gives time and stops volatile losses; ethanol dissolves the lard; removing ethanol helps soap precipitate fully; brine precipitates the soap; cold-water washing removes caustic alkali.

Key questions
